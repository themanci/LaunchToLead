The bigger the monster, the bigger the hero. Learn to paint the pain.

The Problem

Most candidates gloss over the Situation. They say "We had a bug" or "There was a deadline" and rush to the solution. This makes the solution seem easy and unremarkable.

The Solution: Emphasize the pain to make the cure look valuable. High-contrast storytelling makes you memorable.

The Principle: High-Contrast Storytelling

The bigger the monster, the bigger the hero.

If you fought a kitten, nobody cares. If you slayed a dragon, you're a legend. Same skill, different story. Paint the dragon.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Balance: Don't Overdo It

Painting the pain doesn't mean fabricating drama or exaggerating facts. The goal is to surface the REAL stakes that you might be underselling.

  • Be truthful: Only include details you can defend if asked follow-up questions.
  • Be specific: Real numbers and names (even anonymized) are more believable than vague claims.
  • Be proportional: If it was genuinely a small problem, don't make it sound like World War III.

üéØ Quick Reference: Before Every Story

Before you tell ANY behavioral story, ask yourself these 5 questions:

How urgent was the timeline?

What made this harder than normal?

Who was watching or affected?

What was the emotional weight?
