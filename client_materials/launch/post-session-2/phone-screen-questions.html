Strategic Questions That Impress

Never say "I think you covered everything" again. Strategic questions that impress recruiters and help you decide if the role is right for you.

Mansour Manci

Career Coach & Founder, LaunchToLead

LaunchToLead.io

The Problem

"Do you have any questions for me?" ‚Äî The moment most students freeze.

They either say "No, I think you covered everything" or ask something generic like "What does the day-to-day look like?"

The questions you ask in a phone screen tell the recruiter whether you're a passive applicant or a serious candidate who's evaluating opportunities thoughtfully.

Red Flag Questions:

  • "What does the company do?" (shows no research)
  • "How much does this pay?" (too early)
  • "How many vacation days do I get?" (wrong focus)
  • "I don't have any questions" (disengaged)

The Solution: Strategic Question Categories

The Phone Screen Questions to Ask‚Ñ¢ gives you a bank of proven questions organized by what they accomplish. Pick 2-3 questions per call based on what you need to learn.

Goal: Learn what you need to know to evaluate the role AND leave a strong impression
Rule: Always have 2-3 questions prepared, even if some get answered

1

Questions That Show You're Serious

These questions demonstrate you've thought about the role and are evaluating fit, not just collecting offers.

"What does success look like in the first 6 months for this role?"

Shows you're thinking about performance and impact, not just getting a job.

"What's the biggest challenge someone in this role would face early on?"

Shows you're realistic about challenges and want to prepare.

"What skills or qualities separate the top performers in this program?"

Shows you're aiming for excellence, not just participation.

"How does this role contribute to the company's larger goals right now?"

Shows you understand that work exists in a business context.

2

Questions About the Team

Who you work with matters as much as what you do. These questions help you evaluate the team dynamic.

"Can you tell me about the team I'd be working with?"

Gets you info on team size, structure, and who you'd collaborate with.

"How would you describe the management style of the hiring manager?"

Helps you understand if it's hands-on or hands-off leadership.

"What's the typical background of people who do well on this team?"

Tells you if your background aligns with what they value.

"How much collaboration vs. independent work is typical?"

Helps you understand day-to-day work style.

3

Questions About Growth & Development

Show that you're thinking long-term and want to grow, not just punch a clock.

"What does the career path look like for someone who starts in this role?"

Shows you're thinking about long-term fit, not just entry.

"What kind of mentorship or training is available for new hires?"

Signals that you value learning and development.

"How often do people in this role get to work on different types of projects?"

Helps you understand variety and skill development opportunities.

"What technical skills should I focus on developing before starting?"

Shows initiative and that you want to hit the ground running.

4

Questions to Get the "Real Scoop"

These questions cut through the marketing and help you understand what the job is actually like.

"What do you wish you had known before starting at [Company]?"

Gets honest, unscripted insights about the culture.

"What's the most rewarding part of working here? And the most challenging?"

Balanced question that often gets candid responses.

"How has the company changed in the last year or two?"

Reveals whether things are stable, growing, or in flux.

"What's something that surprises new hires about the day-to-day?"

Gets behind the job description to the real experience.

5

Questions About the Process

These questions help you understand what's next and show you're organized and professional.

"What are the next steps in the interview process?"

Standard and expected. Always ask this.

"What's the timeline for making a decision?"

Helps you manage expectations and plan follow-up.

"Is there anything about my background I should address or clarify?"

Gives you a chance to overcome objections on the spot.

"Is there anything else you need from me at this stage?"

Shows you're responsive and eager to move forward.

What NOT to Ask (Yet)

These are valid questions, but save them for later in the process‚Äîideally after you have an offer.

  • ‚Ä¢ "What's the salary range?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "How much PTO do I get?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "Can I work remotely?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "When can I expect a promotion?"
  • ‚Ä¢ "What's your parental leave policy?"

‚úÖ Save for Later Stages

  • ‚Ä¢ Compensation (after offer or in final rounds)
  • ‚Ä¢ Benefits details (HR can provide)
  • ‚Ä¢ Work-from-home policies (later rounds)
  • ‚Ä¢ Start date flexibility (after offer)
  • ‚Ä¢ Relocation assistance (after offer)

üí° Exception: If They Ask About Salary First

If the recruiter brings up compensation, it's fair game to discuss. You can say: "I'm focused on finding the right fit, but I'd love to make sure we're aligned on expectations. What's the range for this role?"

How to Ask Questions Well

1

Have 3-4 questions prepared, expect to ask 2

Some may get answered during the conversation. Always have backups.

2

Listen to their answers‚Äîreally listen

Don't just wait for your turn to speak. Ask follow-up questions.

3

Take notes during the call

This helps you reference their answers in follow-up emails.

4

End with next steps

Always ask about timeline and next steps before hanging up.
