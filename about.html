I'm Not a Career Coach Who Got Lucky Once—I'm an Engineer Who Figured Out The System

Most career advice comes from people who either got incredibly lucky or never actually worked in engineering. I'm neither. I earned 4 promotions in 7 years at John Deere—not because I'm a genius, but because I reverse-engineered the patterns.

I grew up in the Midwest, and honestly, I didn't have some grand master plan to become an engineer. Engineering felt stable, I was decent at math, so... why not? Fast forward to my first role as a mechanical test engineer. I was excited to finally be "doing engineering"—but quickly realized: nobody tells you how to actually build a career.

My 4 Pivots:

Mechanical to Software: Test engineer analyzing engine data → Software engineer writing embedded code

Individual Contributor to Agile Leadership: Software engineer → Product Owner & Scrum Master leading a team

IC to People Manager: Individual contributor → Manager leading 6-10 engineers

Team Manager to Org-Wide Lead: Team manager → Software Development Enablement Lead (company-wide initiatives)

The Turning Point

I started paying attention—not just to my work, but to how the game actually worked. What did the people getting promoted do differently? How did they talk about their work? Who did they know?

What Didn't Work

  • • Just working harder and hoping someone would notice
  • • Waiting for my manager to "advocate" for me
  • • Assuming technical excellence = promotion

What Actually Worked

  • • Building relationships 2-3 levels up
  • • Documenting impact with numbers, not adjectives
  • • Taking on visible problems nobody else wanted

The result? 4 promotions in 7 years, pivoting from mechanical to software to leadership—all at the same company. Not because I'm some genius, but because I figured out the patterns and replicated them.

Why I Do This

I have a full-time job I genuinely enjoy. I don't need Launch to Lead financially. So why do I spend nights and weekends coaching engineers?

Because I remember how lost I felt.

I remember being passed over for promotions, watching peers get ahead, wondering what they knew that I didn't. I want to give you the map—not motivation or platitudes, but the specific, tactical frameworks that worked.

I Didn't Do This Alone (And You Shouldn't Either)

Let me be crystal clear: I did not figure all of this out by myself. Every pivot, every promotion—I had mentors helping me navigate. Not because I was incompetent, but because having someone who's been where you want to go is a superpower, not a crutch.

The Mentors Who Shaped My Career

  • Senior software engineer who taught me to think like a developer
  • Agile coach who showed me the difference between managing tasks vs leading people
  • Director who gave me brutal feedback about my communication style
  • Peer mentors who helped me navigate politics, timing, strategy

Coaching = Leverage

Some people think getting coaching is admitting weakness. That's backwards. Coaching is leverage—tapping into decades of experience and compressing it into YOUR growth.

Without coaching: Try → fail → learn → try again. (2-3 years)

With coaching: Try → feedback → adjust → succeed. (6-12 months)

And here's the thing: I still have coaches and mentors today. I have a leadership coach I meet with monthly. Why? Because the moment you think you've figured it all out is the moment you stop growing.
