Full Script (60-75 Seconds)

[0-12 sec - THE HOOK]
If you're preparing for interviews and you think you need a different story for every possible question...

You're overcomplicating this.

You don't need 50 stories. You don't even need 20.

Here's why 12 is probably enough.

[12-30 sec - THE INSIGHT]
Most interview questions are just asking about the same 7 or 8 competencies in different ways.

Leadership. Teamwork. Problem-solving. Communication. Conflict. Failure. Initiative.

And here's the thing—one good story usually covers 3 or 4 of those at once.

That project where you led a team through a tight deadline? That's leadership. That's teamwork. That's problem-solving. That's working under pressure.

Same story. Four competencies.

[30-50 sec - THE MATH]
So if each of your 12 stories can answer 3-4 different types of questions... do the math.

You've got coverage for 40+ questions with just 12 stories.

And every competency has multiple stories that work for it. So you're never stuck.

[50-75 sec - THE TAKEAWAY]
So here's what you do: Pick your 12 best examples. Write them out. Then go through each one and ask "what else could this answer?"

You'll be shocked how much overlap there is.

When you walk into that interview, you're not scrambling for stories—you're just picking which one fits the question best.

That's the difference between panicking and being prepared.

Performance Notes

The Hook: Stay calm and confident. You're not panicking WITH them—you're the one who figured this out and is saving them stress. "You're overcomplicating this" should feel like relief, not judgment.

The Insight: Slow down when you list the 4 competencies that one story covers. Let them count. "Leadership. Teamwork. Problem-solving. Pressure." Pause. "Same story. Four competencies." This is the lightbulb moment.

The Math: Get a little excited here. "Do the math" should feel like you're revealing something clever. The 40+ coverage number is the payoff.

The Takeaway: Actionable and empowering. They leave knowing exactly what to do. End strong on "panicking vs being prepared" — that's the stickiest line.
