Launch to Lead — Session 2

Interview Day Guide

Everything before, during, and after the interview — from setup to follow-up.

01

Before the Interview

Tech & Environment Check

  • Test camera, microphone, and speakers 15 minutes early
  • Lighting: front-facing, daylight bulbs or ring light — no backlighting or windows behind you
  • Camera: eye level, head + shoulders in frame, not too close or far
  • Audio priority: USB mic > wired earbuds > wireless > never laptop mic
  • Clean, non-distracting background — no clutter, no open doors with people walking by
  • Close unnecessary apps and tabs to prevent notifications
  • Have your phone nearby as a backup if tech fails
  • Have water within reach

Look the Part

  • Dress professionally even for remote interviews — a suit jacket goes a long way on camera
  • Be presentable: hair, grooming, clean background
  • It signals respect and seriousness, regardless of the company's dress code
  • Avoid busy patterns or bright colors on camera — solid colors work best

Have Your Materials Ready

  • Print or pull up your list of interview examples (or resume if you don't have a separate list)
  • Have the job description open for quick reference
  • Write the interviewer's name somewhere visible so you can use it naturally
  • Have a notepad ready for jotting down question keywords
  • If you have visual aids (diagrams, dashboards, before/after slides), have them ready to screen-share
  • Have your reference list accessible — be ready to mention it if the conversation goes well

Mental Prep

  • Research the company: mission, recent news, team structure, culture
  • Review the job description line by line — know which of your stories map to which requirements
  • Map your 3-5 strongest stories to the role's likely competencies (see Story Preparation below)
  • Take 3 deep breaths before joining to settle nerves
  • Smile when the call starts — they can hear it in your voice
  • Remember: they want you to do well. They have an open role to fill.
02

Story Preparation

Don't walk in and hope you'll think of good stories on the spot. Map your experiences to common competencies before the interview so you're ready to pull the right story for any question.

Competency Story Map

Take your 3-5 best experiences and map them to the competencies they can demonstrate. One story can cover multiple competencies.

Team

How it helped your team or changed how they work

Organization

Revenue, cost savings, efficiency — use real numbers

03

When the Interview Starts

Be Transparent About Your Notes

If you have a reference sheet or list of examples, say so right at the start:

"I have a list of my experiences here just for reference — I wanted to make sure I give you the best examples."

This removes any suspicion that you're Googling answers or using AI. Interviewers appreciate the honesty, and it's completely normal. Same goes for your reference list — if you have one ready, it's fine to mention: "I also have a reference list available if you'd like it."

Build Rapport — Be a Person, Not a Robot

You're not a robot answering questions. Be a person they'd want to work with. Match their energy, react genuinely, and find common ground.

Welcome

Open with energy: "Thanks so much for taking the time — I've been looking forward to this."

Acknowledge

React genuinely to what they say. Not just "uh huh" — try "Oh, that's really interesting" or "That makes a lot of sense."

Relate

Find common ground: shared schooling, similar experiences, mutual interests. Don't force it — if it's not natural, skip it.

Mirror

Match their pace and energy — fast talker? Speed up. Calm and deliberate? Ease your pace. Pay attention to their tone.

04

Your Introduction

When they say "Tell me about yourself" — keep it short and simple. The goal is not to eat up time. Save the depth for your behavioral answers.

What to Cover (60–90 seconds):

1

Who you are — your name, your major or current role

2

Your favorite class, project, or work experience — and why it was your favorite

3

Why you're passionate about engineering — what drives you, what excites you about the field

4

What you're looking to get into — the type of role or career you're interested in, with overlap to the role you're interviewing for

Key Principle

Keep it light. Show enthusiasm. Don't deep-dive your resume or over-explain past roles. If they ask curious follow-up questions, answer them — but don't volunteer a 5-minute monologue. Your behavioral answers are where you spend your time.

Common Mistakes in the Intro

Reciting your entire resume chronologically
Talking for 3+ minutes without stopping
Being too vague: "I like solving problems"
Underselling yourself or being self-deprecating
05

Body Language & Presence

On video, body language matters even more because the frame is tight. Small changes make a big difference in how confident and engaged you appear.

Eye Contact

  • Look at your camera lens, not the screen — this is what creates "eye contact" for the interviewer
  • Put a sticky note near your camera as a reminder
  • When listening, it's fine to look at the screen to watch their face — switch back to the camera when you're speaking

Posture & Position

  • Sit up straight — don't slouch or lean back
  • Lean slightly forward when they're speaking to show engagement
  • Keep your shoulders relaxed, not tense
  • Stay centered in the frame — don't drift left/right

Hands & Gestures

  • Use natural hand gestures — they add energy and emphasis
  • Keep hands visible in frame when possible
  • Don't fidget, click pens, or play with things
  • Avoid touching your face repeatedly

Energy & Expression

  • Smile genuinely — especially at the start and when they share something interesting
  • Nod to show you're tracking what they're saying
  • Match their energy level — don't be flat if they're enthusiastic, and vice versa
  • Bring slightly more energy than you think you need — video dampens it
06

Answering Behavioral Questions

STAR+2 Quick Reference

This is covered in detail in your STAR+2 Framework guide. Here's the quick reminder for interview day:

S — Situation

Set the scene. Where, when, what team.

T — Task

Your specific responsibility.

A — Action

What YOU did. Be specific.

R — Result

Outcome + numbers if possible.

+1 — Lessons Learned

What you took away from it.

+2 — If I Could Go Back

What you'd do differently.

Target Time

6-8 minutes per question. Spend the bulk of time on Action and Result. The +2 are what set you apart — they show self-awareness and growth mindset.

Storytelling Tips

DO:

  • Say "I" not "we" — they're evaluating you
  • Use specific numbers: "reduced by 40%" not "improved a lot"
  • Name your role clearly — "I was the lead" or "I was the one who..."
  • Show your decision-making process, not just the final answer
  • Include context on why it mattered — stakes raise interest
  • Vary your tone when telling the story — build toward the result

DON'T:

  • Narrate every detail of the timeline — hit the key points
  • Use jargon the interviewer won't understand
  • Talk about "we" the entire time without clarifying your role
  • Skip the result — "and then it worked out" is not a result
  • Badmouth former colleagues, managers, or companies
  • Use the same story for every question
07

Pausing & Thinking

Pausing is not a weakness. Taking time to think shows you're being thoughtful, not scrambling. Here's what's completely okay to do:

Take a Pause

A 1-minute pause to think is completely fine. Say: "Give me just a minute to think of a good example for that." This is normal and expected. Don't feel rushed.

Ask to Repeat

Always okay to ask them to repeat or rephrase. "Could you say that one more time?" Better to answer the right question well than the wrong question confidently.

Skip and Come Back

If you're stuck, ask to skip and return at the end: "Can we come back to that one? I want to give you a strong answer." If you're still stuck when you circle back, it's perfectly fine to ask for a different question.

Clarify the Competency

If a question could go multiple directions, ask: "Is this more about [aspect A] or [aspect B]?" This ensures you give them the answer they're actually looking for — and it shows you're thinking critically.

The Power Pause

Intentional pausing is a sign of confidence, not uncertainty. Use it strategically:

  • Before answering: A 3-5 second pause after they ask shows you're considering your best answer. It's the opposite of rambling.
  • Before your Result: A brief pause before sharing the outcome builds anticipation and gives it weight.
  • After a key number: "We reduced defects by 60%." [pause] — Let the number land.
08

Time Management

Track Your Time

  • Use a stopwatch or timer — your phone face-up on the desk or the Windows clock are fine
  • It's not weird — it shows you're organized
  • Reset the timer at the start of each answer
  • If you're past 8 minutes, wrap up the Result and keep the +2 brief
  • If you're under 4 minutes, you're probably not giving enough detail in Action

Ask How Many Questions

It's perfectly fine — and smart — to ask:

"I'd like to manage my time well — how many questions are we covering today?"

This shows you're organized and time-conscious. You can then pace yourself: if there are 4 questions in 60 minutes, you have roughly 12-15 minutes per question (including their follow-ups). That's plenty of time for STAR+2.

09

Delivery Tips

Voice & Tone

  • Vary your tone — monotone kills engagement
  • Emphasize key words and numbers: "We saved fifteen thousand dollars per quarter"
  • Don't rush — confidence has a pace
  • Pause before key results for emphasis
  • Smile while talking — it changes your tone even on audio-only calls
  • For phone calls, stand up — it projects more energy in your voice

Active Listening

  • Wait 1 second after they finish before you respond — don't cut them off
  • React to what they say — engage, don't just wait for your turn
  • Use their name 2-3 times naturally throughout the interview
  • If they share something personal (weekend plans, sports), engage with it briefly
  • Nod and give verbal affirmations: "Right," "That makes sense," "Absolutely"

When You Don't Know

  • Be honest — never fake an answer
  • "I'm not sure of the exact answer, but my approach would be to..."
  • "I haven't encountered that specific scenario, but a related experience I had was..."
  • Intellectual honesty > false confidence — interviewers can tell when you're making it up

Visual Aids

If you have diagrams, before/after comparisons, or results dashboards from your projects — bring them. It transforms you from "someone who said smart things" into "the one who showed me their work."

  • Virtual: "I have a quick visual — mind if I share my screen for 30 seconds?"
  • In-person: Printed one-pagers in a folder. Pull them out casually.
  • Keep them simple — one slide, one chart, one comparison. Not a presentation.
10

"Do You Have Any Questions?"

Don't Ask These in the Interview

These are HR/process questions. Ask them in your follow-up email to the recruiter — not to the hiring manager or team members.

Benefits Start date Relocation Salary Vacation Hiring timeline Anything you could Google

Ask Questions That Let You Show More Value

This is your second chance to demonstrate fit. Don't waste it on logistics. Ask questions that open the door for you to share more of your experience.

The Best Question — The Gap Closer

"Knowing what you've learned about me today — what would you say is one of my biggest gaps or lacking experiences compared to others who were successful in this role, or something that might make it hard for me to succeed?"

When they answer, you close the gap with an experience they didn't hear about: "That's interesting — I actually didn't get to share my experience with [X], which I think is relevant here because..."

You're turning a potential weakness into a strength. You're also showing that you're coachable, self-aware, and not afraid of feedback.

Other Strong Questions

These show genuine interest in the role and the team, not just yourself:

  • "What does success look like in the first 90 days?"
  • "What's the biggest challenge on this team right now?"
  • "What do you wish you'd known when you started in this role?"
  • "How does the team collaborate on a day-to-day basis?"
  • "What's the career growth path for someone in this position?"
  • "What do you enjoy most about working here?"
  • "How does this team measure success?"
  • "What would be the first project or problem I'd be working on?"
11

Dealbreakers

Never Bring Up Dealbreakers Before You Have an Offer

If the role requires relocating somewhere you don't want to go, has a schedule you can't work with, or includes something you'd never agree to — do not bring it up during the interview. Not in the phone screen, not in the behavioral round, not in the Q&A.

If they ask directly, be agreeable and redirect:

"For right now, I'm really just focused on making sure I'm a fit for the role and the role is a fit for me. I'm sure we can work out the details if there's an offer extended."

Why This Matters

  • Raising concerns early puts you on the "difficult" or "high maintenance" list before they even know your value
  • You don't know how strong a candidate you are yet — you could be far and away their top choice
  • If you're their #1 pick and candidate #2 is significantly weaker, they may be willing to negotiate on things you assumed were non-negotiable
  • You have leverage when an offer is extended, not before

Bottom Line

The time to negotiate is after they've decided they want you. That's when you have maximum leverage. Before that, your only job is to be the best candidate in the room.

Common Topics to Defer Until After an Offer

Relocation requirements
Salary / compensation
Remote vs. on-site
Travel requirements
Start date conflicts
Shift / schedule preferences
12

Closing the Interview

End Strong (30-60 seconds)

The last impression matters as much as the first. Don't just trail off — close deliberately.

  • Express genuine enthusiasm — mention something specific from the conversation that excited you
  • Reiterate your interest: "I'm really excited about this role, especially after hearing about [specific thing]."
  • Express confidence without arrogance: "I'm confident I can make a real impact here."
  • Confirm next steps: "What should I expect from here?"
  • Thank them sincerely for their time

"This has been a great conversation — I'm even more excited about this role after hearing about [specific thing you discussed]. I'm confident in my ability to contribute here, and I'd love the opportunity to join the team. Thank you for your time."

13

After the Interview

Thank-You Email (Within 24 Hours)

Sending one email to the whole interview panel is fine — no need to send individual emails to each person.

  • Highlight your interest in the role — what specifically excites you about it
  • Explain why you like the role — reference something specific from the conversation
  • Reinforce why you're a good fit — connect your experience to what they need
  • Drop your references — "I'd also like to share a few references who can speak to my work: [names/contact info]"
  • Keep it brief — 5-7 sentences
  • Proofread carefully — typos undermine credibility

Self-Debrief (Same Day)

While it's fresh, write down:

  • Which questions went well and which felt weak
  • What stories you used — so you don't repeat them in the next round
  • Any follow-up items you promised ("I'll send you that link")
  • What you'd do differently next time
  • Key things the interviewer shared about the role or team

If You Don't Get the Offer

Respond gracefully. Their first choice might not work out. Other roles might open up. The hiring manager might move companies and remember you. Always leave the door open.

Thank them for the process and their time
Express interest in future opportunities
Ask for feedback: "Even a sentence or two would help me improve"
Connect on LinkedIn to stay on their radar
14

Phone Screen Tips

Phone screens are usually 20-30 minutes with a recruiter or hiring manager. They're shorter and more conversational than full behavioral interviews, but they're still an evaluation. Treat them seriously.

Phone-Specific Tips

  • Stand up when you take the call — it projects more energy
  • Smile while talking — they can hear it even on the phone
  • Have your resume printed and in front of you
  • Use a quiet room with no background noise
  • Ask for the interviewer's email at the end so you can send a thank-you
  • Have a pen and paper, not a keyboard — typing sounds are distracting

Common Phone Screen Questions

  • Fit: "Why this company?" "Why this role?" — have specific reasons ready
  • Background: "Walk me through your resume" — 2-3 minute version, not your full intro
  • Availability / Relocation / Salary: Deflect all of these — "I'm flexible and really just focused on finding the right fit. I'm sure we can work out the details if there's an offer extended." (See section 11 — Dealbreakers)
  • Interest level: "Where else are you interviewing?" — be honest about having options without playing games
15

Special Scenarios

Panel Interviews

  • Write down every panelist's name and role at the start
  • Direct your answer to the person who asked, but make eye contact with the whole group
  • Use panelists' names when responding: "That's a great question, Sarah"
  • Pay attention to non-verbal cues from quiet panelists — they're still evaluating you
  • Send individual thank-you emails to each panelist afterward

Technical Interviews

  • Think out loud — they care about your process, not just the answer
  • Ask clarifying questions before diving in: "Can I assume...?"
  • If you're stuck, explain what you've tried and where you're blocked
  • It's okay to say "I'd look this up in practice" — they know nobody memorizes everything
  • If they give hints, take them — it shows you're coachable, not weak

Group Activities or Case Interviews

  • Lead without dominating — invite quieter people in: "What do you think, [name]?"
  • Build on others' ideas rather than always pushing your own
  • Volunteer for roles like timekeeper or note-taker — it shows initiative
  • Be collaborative, not competitive — they're watching how you work with others

Informal / Social Rounds

  • These are still an evaluation — stay professional
  • Ask about their experience, not just the company
  • Don't over-drink if it's a dinner interview
  • Be curious and engaged — show genuine interest in the people, not just the job
  • These are often "culture fit" assessments — be yourself, but the professional version
