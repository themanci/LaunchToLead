Block 2: The Psychology of Influence

"Before we talk about WHAT to say, let's understand WHY it works."

Today we're going to master the promotion conversation. But first — a framework that will change how you approach every important conversation for the rest of your career.

What most people do:

Walk into a promotion conversation focused on what they want to say.

What top performers do:

Walk in focused on how their message will be received — at a subconscious level.

The Driver & The Caveman

Every person in a conversation has two minds operating simultaneously.

🧠

The Driver

(Conscious Mind)

  • • Logical, rational, analytical
  • • Evaluates facts and evidence
  • • Considers pros and cons
  • • This is who you think you're talking to
🦴

The Caveman

(Subconscious Mind)

  • • Instinctive, protective, reactive
  • • Fight-or-flight decisions in milliseconds
  • • Projects past experiences onto new situations
  • • This is who is actually making the decision

When you walk into your manager's office to talk promotion, you're not talking to one person.

You're talking to their Driver AND their Caveman.

The Iceberg Model

Above the Surface

What they SAY and DO

Words, actions, logical responses

~ ~ ~ WATERLINE ~ ~ ~

Below the Surface

What they FEEL and BELIEVE

Past experiences, biases, instincts, fears, first impressions

This part is BIGGER and makes the real decisions.

Applied to your promotion conversation:

Your manager's logical mind evaluates your evidence. But their subconscious already has an impression of you — built from every interaction, email, and meeting over months.

The Titanic Lesson:

By the time you see the iceberg above water, the collision below the surface has already happened. By the time you sit down to talk promotion, your manager's subconscious has already formed its opinion.

The Good News:

You've been programming their iceberg positively since Session 1 — Echo Emails, Gravity Leader connections, recognition. That's the foundation we're building on today.

The 3 Laws of Influence

1

You influence ALWAYS.

Every second of every interaction, you're either influencing positively or negatively. There is no neutral. Are you aware of the influence you're having — in meetings, in emails, in hallway conversations?

2

Small things count just as much as big things.

We nail the big moments — the presentation, the project delivery. But the small things can undo it all: the late reply, the sigh in the meeting, the name you forgot. The subconscious doesn't weigh them differently.

3

Influence on the subconscious dominates.

The first impression — positive or negative — acts like a magnet. It attracts more of the same. If your manager's Caveman has a positive baseline on you, they'll look for more reasons to say yes. If negative, they'll find reasons to say no.

Source: Hansen Beck Expedition Logs — Influence

Same Person, Same Ask — Different Outcome

❌ Scenario A

You walk in stressed. You've been rehearsing what you're going to say. You're defensive before the conversation even starts.

First words: "So, I've been here for 3 years and I think it's time we talk about my promotion."

Your manager's Caveman immediately goes on guard: "Here we go. Another person asking me for something."

Result:

Every following point you make gets filtered through a negative lens. Even good evidence gets dismissed or minimized.

✅ Scenario B

You walk in genuinely positive. You asked yourself: "What do I like about this person? Why am I enjoying myself?" before the meeting.

First words: "I've been thinking about how I can contribute more to the team. Can we talk about what the next level looks like?"

Your manager's Caveman relaxes: "This person wants to help. I like that energy."

Result:

Every following point gets a fair hearing. The first positive impulse attracts more positives — Law #3 in action.

You only get one chance to set the tone. The first 10 seconds program their iceberg.

Block 3: The Gap Analysis Contract™

🎯 Goal

Get your manager to put the promotion criteria on paper. No more vague goals. No more "you're just not ready yet."

❌ Without a Gap Analysis

  • • "Keep doing great work"
  • • "You're not quite ready, but getting close"
  • • "We'll revisit next cycle"
  • • Goalposts keep moving
  • • You have no idea what to focus on

✅ With a Gap Analysis

  • • Specific, named gaps to close
  • • Written down, mutually agreed
  • • Monthly check-in cadence
  • • Evidence-based progress tracking
  • • Your manager becomes your ALLY
13
LaunchToLead
Gap Analysis Contract™ + Service-Based Script™

The Script

"I really enjoy what I do here, and I want to keep growing with the team. I've been thinking about what it would take for me to contribute at the next level. Could we map out the specific areas where I'd need to grow? I'd love to have a clear picture so I can be intentional about my development — and so we can check in on progress together."

🧠 Iceberg Programming

Opens with genuine enthusiasm (positive first impulse). Positions the ask as contribution, not extraction.

📋 Service-Based Framing

"I want to contribute more" not "I deserve a promotion." Their Caveman relaxes — you're offering help, not demanding.

🤝 Collaborative Ask

"Check in on progress together" — you're a TEAM. This makes your manager an ally, not a gatekeeper.

🔑 The Key Follow-Up:

"Would it be helpful to write this down so we both have a reference? I can send a summary after our conversation."

→ This is how you get the "contract." You summarize, they confirm. Now it exists on paper.

The Promotion Conversation

Service-Based Script Library™ + Promotion Positioning Philosophy™

"I've been reflecting on where I can have the most impact on the team. I feel like I've grown a lot in [specific areas], and I'm starting to operate at the next level in [specific examples].   • • Anything that sounds like an ultimatum

Block 5: The Objection Playbook™

🎯 Goal

You WILL get pushback. That's normal. The key is having a prepared, calm response — not a defensive reaction.

🧠 The Caveman Lens:

When your manager pushes back, ask yourself: Is this their Driver giving me real feedback? Or is this their Caveman protecting themselves? Objections like "budgets are frozen" might be real — or might be their Caveman avoiding an uncomfortable commitment. Your job is to respond to BOTH.

17
LaunchToLead

Objection Handling

1

"We're not doing promotions right now."

Your response:

"I completely understand. I'm not asking for a promotion today — I'm asking what it would take when the time comes. Can we map out the gaps so I'm ready when the window opens?"

→ Reframes from "give me" to "help me prepare." Their Caveman relaxes because you removed the pressure.

2

"You're doing great, keep it up."

Your response:

"I appreciate that — it means a lot. Can I ask what specific things I would need to demonstrate to move to the next level? I want to make sure my growth is intentional, not just general."

→ Turns vague praise into a concrete conversation. Forces specificity without confrontation.

3

"You're not ready yet."

Your response:

"I hear you. Can you help me understand specifically what 'ready' looks like? I want to close those gaps as fast as I can, and having clear targets would help me get there."

→ Shows humility (Caveman at ease) while demanding specificity (Driver engaged). This is the Gap Analysis pivot.

4

"There's no headcount / budget for a promotion."

Your response:

"I understand the constraints. Could we put together a plan so that when headcount opens up, I'm first in line? And is there anything else I can do in the meantime — a title change, expanded scope, or leading a project?"

→ Accepts reality gracefully. Asks for alternatives. Shows you're solution-oriented, not entitled.

5

"We need to see more consistency / more time."

Your response:

"That's fair. Could we set a specific timeframe and criteria? If I demonstrate [X] over the next [3-6 months], would that be enough to revisit? I want to make sure we're both clear on what success looks like."

→ Converts vague resistance into a measurable agreement. This IS the Gap Analysis Contract in action.

Block 6: The Headache Fixer Strategy™

"Find out what keeps your manager up at night — then fix it."

Your manager has problems they need solved. If YOU become the person who solves them, you become indispensable. And indispensable people get promoted.

How to Find Their Headaches:

  • → Listen in 1:1s — what frustrates them?
  • → What keeps coming up in team meetings as blockers?
  • → What processes are broken and nobody owns?
  • → What does their manager pressure them about?

How to Fix Them:

  • → Build the tool, fix the process, write the doc
  • → Do it without being asked
  • → Present it as a gift, not a resume bullet
  • → Use the Scope Beacon™ — communicate you did it

Iceberg Rule #3 in Action: Every headache you fix = another +1 on the abacus. Their subconscious starts associating YOU with relief, solutions, and trust. The scale tips.

Managing Up Masterclass™

The 4 Rules of Managing Up

1. Make Them Look Good

When meeting with skip-level leadership, your manager's name should come up positively. Give them credit. If they look good, they'll make YOU look good.

2. No Surprises

Deliver bad news fast and early. Always with context and proposed solutions — never just the problem. Their Caveman trusts you because you're not hiding things.

3. Handle Disagreements with Grace

Push back when you need to — but privately, not in group settings. Frame it as "here's another perspective" not "you're wrong." Protect the relationship above the argument.

4. Fix Their Headaches

Find the friction in their life and remove it. Tools, docs, processes — whatever nobody else wants to own. This is how you become irreplaceable.

Key Insight: Managing up isn't manipulation. It's building a relationship where your manager wants to advocate for you — because you make their life better.

Executive Presence: A Preview

We'll go deep in Session 3. For now, two principles to start practicing immediately.

Say/Do Ratio™

Do what you said you would. Every time.

  • → Don't commit to more than you can deliver
  • → Deliver what you committed, fast and well
  • → A reputation for "getting things done" IS executive presence

🧠 Every kept promise = +1 on the iceberg. Every broken one = -1.

Executive Presence Blueprint™

Be serious about results. People follow that energy.

  • → Show passion for outcomes, not just process
  • → Balance — not always joking, not always intense
  • → Never complain down. Complain up. Shield your team.

🧠 People's Cavemen follow leaders who seem CALM, CONFIDENT, and FOCUSED.
