The "Hard Work = Reward" Fallacy‚Ñ¢ Block 1

The Belief: "If I just put my head down, work hard, and deliver great results, I'll get promoted."

The Reality: Engineers who grind for 5-10 years without seeking visibility get labeled as "the X guy who loves that work" and passed over. Hard work is the prerequisite, not the differentiator. Consider: promotion every 8 years = 4-5 promotions over a career. Promotion every 2-3 years = 10-15+. The difference in compensation and influence is massive.

The New Belief: Visibility and intentionality accelerate the timeline. Hard work alone is necessary but not sufficient.

The "I Shouldn't Have to Self-Promote" Delusion‚Ñ¢ Block 1

The Belief: "My work should speak for itself. Self-promotion is sleazy."

The Reframe: Who cares about your career advancement more than you do? Maybe your grandma‚Äîbut not anyone at work. Nobody else will be as consistent or emotionally invested in YOUR visibility as you. This isn't sleazy politician behavior‚Äîit's making influential people aware of your abilities so you can add MORE value with broader influence.

The Rules: ‚úÖ Be your own biggest advocate. ‚úÖ Make people aware of genuine value. ‚ùå Never misrepresent or stretch the truth.

The "Politics is Dirty" Misconception‚Ñ¢ Block 1

The Belief: "I don't want to play politics. Politics is manipulative and dirty."

The Reality: Politics is dirty IF you play dirty‚Äîlying, slandering, manipulating. Politics itself is simply: getting visibility on your value, building relationships that matter, and positioning yourself for more influence. You can refuse to "play politics" and stay stuck, or play strategically, ethically, and morally‚Äîand advance.

The Choice: The game exists whether you play or not. Choosing not to play just means you lose by default.

The Green Light Audit‚Ñ¢ Block 2

What It Is: A 14-question diagnostic that reveals whether you have the "green light" conditions for promotion.

What It Checks: Manager relationship quality, skip-level visibility, career goal clarity, promotion conversation recency, perception as go-to person, cross-functional visibility, next-level work demonstration, and more.

How We Use It: Review their yes/no answers, identify the 2-3 biggest gaps, and prioritize which to fix first. Most common gap: no skip-level relationship.

The Role Saturation Threshold‚Ñ¢ (85% Rule) Block 2

The Principle: You should already be doing 85% of the next level's work BEFORE asking for promotion.

Why It Matters: If you're only doing 50% of next-level work, you're asking for a bet. At 85%, you're asking for recognition of reality. This is the paradox: prove you're at the next level to GET to the next level.

How We Use It: Assess where they stand. If below 85%, identify what's missing and what opportunities exist to close the gap.

The Skip-Level Request Protocol‚Ñ¢ Block 3

What It Is: The exact script and approach for requesting a meeting with your manager's manager‚Äîwithout seeming like you're going over your boss's head.

Key Elements: (1) Loop in your direct manager first for transparency. (2) Frame the ask around learning and adding value, not career advancement. (3) Request 20-30 minutes‚Äîlow commitment. (4) Suggest flexibility on timing.

Why It Works: Most skip-levels say yes. They want to know who's on their team. The ask itself signals ambition and initiative.

The Skip-Level Meeting Playbook‚Ñ¢ Block 3

What to Do: Once you're in the meeting, execute this playbook for maximum impact.

The Agenda: (1) Ask about their priorities‚Äîwhat keeps them up at night? (2) Share one thing you're working on that aligns. (3) Ask: "What would make someone at my level really stand out to you?" (4) End with: "I'd love to do this periodically if you're open to it."

The Goal: Plant a seed. Make them remember your name. Get permission to do it again. One meeting becomes a relationship.

The Echo Email‚Ñ¢ Block 4

What It Is: A weekly email to your manager (and eventually skip-level) that broadcasts wins, progress, and blockers. The core recurring visibility system.

The Format: Subject: "Weekly Update - [Name] - [Date]". Body: Wins (accomplished this week), In Progress (working on), Blockers (need help with), Next Week (focused on). Send Friday afternoon.

Why It Works: (1) Creates a paper trail of your contributions. (2) Makes your manager's job easier‚Äîthey report up too. (3) Becomes raw material for performance reviews and promotion cases.

The Recognition Boomerang‚Ñ¢ Block 4

The Principle: A good leader takes all the blame when things go wrong and gives all the credit when things go right.

How It Works: When you publicly defer credit to others, you actually look BETTER. People know who did what. By not verbally claiming credit, you avoid arrogance while still being associated with the win. The credit "boomerangs" back to you.

Application: In Echo Emails and public communications, highlight others' contributions. Tag teammates. Share credit broadly. This builds social capital and paradoxically increases YOUR reputation.

Organic Broadcasting‚Ñ¢ Block 4

What It Is: Sharing wins in a way that feels natural, not braggy. Weaving accomplishments into normal conversation and documentation without sounding like you're selling yourself.

Techniques: (1) Attach wins to status updates‚Äî"While working on X, we also achieved Y." (2) Frame as learning‚Äî"Interesting finding from the project..." (3) CC strategically‚Äîinclude relevant stakeholders naturally. (4) Use Slack/Teams for quick visibility hits.

The Test: If it feels uncomfortable, you're probably in the right zone. Most people under-broadcast, not over.

The Gravity Audit‚Ñ¢ Block 5

What It Is: Identifying the 3-5 "Gravity Leaders"‚Äîpeople who have outsized influence over your promotion, reputation, or career trajectory.

Who Qualifies: Skip-level managers, leaders of high-visibility projects, cross-functional partners who influence your domain, executives who run key initiatives, respected senior ICs who have the ear of leadership.

The Strategy: For each Gravity Leader: (1) Identify an entry point‚Äîhow to get on their radar. (2) Determine value-add‚Äîwhat can you offer them? (3) Set a timeline‚Äîwhen will you make first contact? One meaningful interaction per month is enough.

Practice articulating your unique value in 60 seconds to a senior leader who asks "What do you do?" We'll craft your personal pitch that highlights impact, not just job duties. You'll be ready for any elevator conversation or networking opportunity.

Setup: You play a VP at a company event asking "So what do you do?"

Goal: 60-second pitch that makes them want to learn more.
