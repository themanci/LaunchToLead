Session 3: Live Mock Interview & Refinement

90-Minute Session | 6 Segments | Days 17-21

Quick Jump: Pre-Session Briefing Mock Interview Debrief Post-Session

📋 Session Overview

Session Goals

  • ✓ Conduct a realistic 45-minute mock interview
  • ✓ Provide specific, actionable feedback on performance
  • ✓ Reinforce rejection reframe and burnout prevention systems
  • ✓ Preview negotiation strategies for when offers come
  • ✓ Send client off confident and interview-ready

What Client Should Bring

  • □ Target job posting for mock interview simulation
  • □ Completed Interview Cheat Sheet (from Session 2)
  • □ Master Story Vault with 10+ detailed stories

Pre-Interview Briefing

Minutes 0-10 | Slides 3-4

🎤 Opening Script

"Great to see you! This is the big one—your full mock interview. In a few minutes, we're going to shift into interview mode. I'll be playing the role of an interviewer at [Company/Role]. Before we start, let's do a quick mindset check. Remember: this is a conversation to explore mutual fit—not a test you pass or fail. You're evaluating them too. You've prepared your stories, you know your value. Trust the work you've done. Any questions before we begin? Once we start, treat this exactly like a real interview. Ready?"

Key Points to Cover

  • • Set the right mindset (conversation, not interrogation)
  • • Review the target role/company for the mock
  • • Answer any last questions before "interview mode"
  • • Confirm they have their Interview Cheat Sheet ready

💡 Facilitator Tip

If client seems nervous, normalize it: "Nerves mean you care—that's good energy. Channel it into enthusiasm." If they didn't send a job posting, pick a realistic generic role in their target industry.

Transition to Mock Interview

"Alright, let's begin. [Pause, shift demeanor] Hi, thanks for joining us today. I'm [Name], and I'll be interviewing you for the [Position] role. Tell me a little about yourself..."

2

Full Mock Interview

Minutes 10-75 | 65 Minutes

Opening (5 min) → Background (5 min) → Behavioral Questions (45 min) → Job Fit (5 min) → Candidate Q&A (5 min)

💡 Question Selection Strategy

Target 5 behavioral questions in 45 minutes (~9 min each including follow-ups). Choose questions from different competencies based on the client's target role and areas needing practice. Always include at least one "negative" question (failure, mistake, conflict).

🎯 Behavioral Question Bank

Select 5 questions across different competencies. Check the boxes for questions you'll use in today's mock interview.

1. Leadership & Initiative

Assessing: Taking ownership, stepping up, driving results without being asked

✅ What to Look For

  • • Identifies problems proactively, doesn't wait to be told
  • • Takes ownership of outcomes, not just tasks
  • • Influences others without formal authority
  • • Shows clear "I did" vs "we did" — knows their specific contribution
  • • Demonstrates follow-through to measurable results
  • • Volunteers for stretch assignments or ambiguous situations

🚩 Red Flags

  • • Waits for permission or explicit instructions
  • • Takes credit for team efforts ("we" without clarifying their role)
  • • Confuses activity with leadership (busy ≠ leading)
  • • Can't articulate the impact of their initiative
  • • Blames others when initiative fails
  • • Leadership examples are all about authority, not influence

2. Problem-Solving & Technical Judgment

Assessing: Analytical thinking, debugging approach, technical decision-making

✅ What to Look For

  • • Structured approach: breaks down complex problems systematically
  • • Considers multiple solutions before deciding
  • • Articulates trade-offs clearly (cost, time, quality, risk)
  • • Uses data/evidence to support decisions
  • • Knows when to ask for help vs. push through
  • • Learns from debugging experiences—doesn't repeat mistakes

🚩 Red Flags

  • • Jumps to solutions without understanding the problem
  • • Can't explain their reasoning or thought process
  • • "Trial and error" without hypothesis-driven approach
  • • Overcomplicates simple problems or oversimplifies complex ones
  • • Blames tools, time, or others for technical failures
  • • No mention of validation or testing decisions

3. Teamwork & Collaboration

Assessing: Working with others, cross-functional skills, supporting teammates

✅ What to Look For

  • • Shares credit genuinely—highlights teammates' contributions
  • • Adapts communication style for different audiences
  • • Seeks to understand before being understood (active listening)
  • • Puts team success above personal recognition
  • • Offers help proactively, not just when asked
  • • Handles conflict constructively—focuses on solutions, not blame

🚩 Red Flags

  • • "I" language only—no acknowledgment of team
  • • Speaks negatively about previous teammates/managers
  • • Avoids conflict entirely OR escalates unnecessarily
  • • Takes a "not my job" attitude
  • • Can't give specific examples of helping others
  • • Blames team dynamics for personal failures

4. Communication & Influence

Assessing: Explaining complex ideas, persuading stakeholders, presenting

✅ What to Look For

  • • Adjusts message complexity for the audience
  • • Uses concrete examples, analogies, or visuals
  • • Listens and addresses objections thoughtfully
  • • Shows preparation and structure in presentations
  • • Delivers difficult messages with empathy and clarity
  • • Persuades through logic AND relationship-building

🚩 Red Flags

  • • Uses jargon when explaining to non-experts
  • • Rambles without clear structure or point
  • • Gets defensive when challenged
  • • Avoids difficult conversations entirely
  • • Relies on authority/position rather than persuasion
  • • Can't give examples of adapting communication style

5. Adaptability & Learning

Assessing: Handling change, learning quickly, dealing with ambiguity

✅ What to Look For

  • • Embraces change as opportunity, not threat
  • • Has a systematic approach to learning new things
  • • Stays calm under uncertainty—takes action anyway
  • • Seeks out unfamiliar challenges proactively
  • • Shows curiosity and asks good questions
  • • Quickly integrates new information into their approach

🚩 Red Flags

  • • Resists change or complains about shifting priorities
  • • "That's not what I was hired to do" attitude
  • • Freezes when facing ambiguity—needs all answers first
  • • Relies only on familiar methods, won't try new approaches
  • • Learning approach is passive (waiting to be taught)
  • • Gets frustrated easily when things don't go as planned

6. Failure & Self-Awareness ⭐ MUST ASK AT LEAST ONE

Assessing: Humility, self-reflection, growth mindset, accountability

✅ What to Look For (Strong Answers)

  • • Owns the failure fully—no deflection or excuses
  • • Shows genuine self-reflection, not scripted humility
  • • Describes specific actions taken to improve
  • • Shares a REAL failure, not a humble-brag
  • • Demonstrates growth—behavior actually changed
  • • Comfortable discussing imperfection (emotionally mature)

🚩 Red Flags (Weak Answers)

  • • Blames others, circumstances, or timing
  • • "Failure" is actually a disguised success
  • • Can't name a genuine failure (lack of self-awareness)
  • • Gets defensive or uncomfortable with the topic
  • • Learning is generic: "I learned to work harder"
  • • No evidence the lesson was actually applied

7. Drive & Motivation

Assessing: Passion for engineering, career goals, intrinsic motivation

✅ What to Look For

  • • Genuine enthusiasm—eyes light up talking about work
  • • Clear career direction with logical reasoning
  • • Pursues learning outside of job requirements
  • • Connects personal interests to professional goals
  • • Shows curiosity about the role and company specifically
  • • Has researched the company and asks thoughtful questions

🚩 Red Flags

  • • Generic answers: "I like solving problems"
  • • Motivation is purely extrinsic (salary, title, prestige)
  • • No side projects, learning, or personal development
  • • Can't articulate why THIS role vs. any other
  • • Career goals seem unrealistic or disconnected
  • • No questions about the role, team, or company

Total Questions: 50+ across 7 competencies | Red questions = Negative/Stress questions

📋 Running the Mock Interview

⚠️ Facilitator Conduct During Interview

  • • Stay in character — Act like a real interviewer
  • • Take notes — Capture specifics for debrief
  • • Use follow-ups — "Tell me more about..." "What was the result?"
  • • Watch non-verbals — Eye contact, posture, energy, filler words
  • • Don't coach during — Save all feedback for debrief

Ending the Mock Interview

"Those are all the questions I have for now. Do you have any questions for me?" [Answer 2-3 of their questions in character, then close] "Great, thanks for your time today. We'll be in touch."

Immediate Debrief

Minutes 75-90 | Slides 5-6

🎤 Debrief Script

"Alright, let's step out of interview mode. Nice work! Before I share my observations, I want to hear from you—how did that feel? What moments felt strong? What caught you off guard?" [Let client self-assess first] "Great self-awareness. Here's what I noticed..."

Debrief Flow

  1. Break character clearly
  2. Self-assessment first (let them reflect)
  3. Share 3-4 specific positives
  4. Share improvements (specific, actionable)
  5. Summarize top 3 action items

⚠️ Debrief Best Practices

  • • Be specific: "When you answered X, you..."
  • • Balance: 60% positive, 40% constructive
  • • Make it actionable: "Next time, try..."
  • • Don't overwhelm: Focus on top 3
  • • End on a high note

Rejection Reframe + Burnout Prevention

Minutes 75-82 | Slides 7-8

🎤 Mental Resilience Script

"Before we wrap up, I want to talk about the mental game. The job search is a marathon, not a sprint. You're going to face rejection—everyone does. The difference is how you process it. The Rejection Reframe System is about treating the job search like a debugging exercise. Track everything. When you get rejected, look for patterns. Are you getting callbacks but not advancing? That tells you where to focus. You're not failing—you're gathering data. And for Burnout Prevention: set a daily goal—maybe 5 applications—then STOP. Guilt-free rest is part of the system."

Rejection Reframe Key Points

  • • Track rejections as data, not failure
  • • Identify bottlenecks (resume → phone → interview)
  • • Decouple self-worth from outcomes
  • • "You're debugging, not failing"

Burnout Prevention Key Points

  • • Set daily goals, then STOP
  • • Track streaks for motivation
  • • Schedule rest days (guilt-free)
  • • Log lessons learned daily
5

Offer & Negotiation Overview

Minutes 82-87 | Slides 9-10

🎤 Negotiation Script

"Quick preview on negotiation: yes, entry-level can negotiate. Companies expect it. Even if base salary is fixed, there's often flexibility on signing bonus, start date, PTO, remote work, and more. Before you get an offer, do your research. Know your three numbers: the minimum you'll accept, your target, and your high-end ask. The materials have scripts for exactly what to say. When you get an offer—reach out to me. We'll review it together."

Key Points to Cover

  • • Entry-level CAN negotiate (signing bonus, start date, PTO, remote)
  • • Know your three numbers: Walk-Away, Target, Bolstering
  • • Research before you get an offer
  • • Scripts are in the materials—practice them

💡 Facilitator Tip

Emphasize that they should reach out when they get an offer. Many new grads rush to accept without negotiating. Offer to review with them before they respond.

Final Prep & Send-off

Minutes 87-90 | Slides 11-12

🎤 Closing Script

"That wraps up our three sessions. You now have everything you need: a targeted resume, powerful stories, interview frameworks, negotiation scripts, and mental resilience systems. For your real interviews: test your tech beforehand, review your stories, take a few deep breaths, and remember—it's a conversation. You're evaluating them too. After each interview, send a thank-you email within 24 hours. Reference something specific from the conversation. Most importantly: you're ready. Trust the work you've done. Go get that offer—and reach out when you do. I'll be cheering you on."

Next Steps Reminder

  • ✓ Apply consistently using the burnout prevention system
  • ✓ Track everything using your application tracker
  • ✓ Practice stories before each interview
  • ✓ Research salary before negotiating
  • ✓ Reach out when you get an offer

📤 Post-Session Checklist

🔧 Common Situations & Responses

"I keep freezing on behavioral questions."

Use the Power Pause script: "That's a great question, let me think of the most relevant example." Practice scanning your Story Vault during the pause.

"My stories sound too long/rambling."

Aim for 90-120 seconds per story. Use the wrap-around script: "Does that answer your question, or would you like me to go deeper on any part?"

"I don't know how to ask for a higher salary."

The scripts are in the negotiation materials. Practice: "I'm excited about this role. Based on my research and the value I'll bring, I was hoping for something closer to [Target Number]."

"I'm getting interviews but no offers."

You've passed the resume screen—that's good. Focus on story delivery, enthusiasm, and connection. Practice with others and review recordings of yourself.
