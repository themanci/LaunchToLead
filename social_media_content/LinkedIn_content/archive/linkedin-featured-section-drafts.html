How I Got 4 Promotions in 7 Years at John Deere

(Without being the smartest coder in the room)

THE PROBLEM

Universities teach you to calculate.
They don't teach you to navigate.

So you:

  • Work hard
  • Hit your deadlines
  • Hope your boss notices
  • Watch others get promoted

Sound familiar?

I was stuck there too. Until I realized:

Performance is not the differentiator.
Performance is the ENTRY FEE.

THE SYSTEM I BUILT

I started treating my career like an engineering problem:

  • Identify the variables (Visibility, Impact, Sponsorship)
  • Optimize the constraints
  • Solve for X (Velocity)

Here's what I learned:

1. THE IMPACT EQUATION

Stop listing tasks. Start proving value.

Old: "Maintained spreadsheets"
New: "Automated tracking for 3 programs, cutting time by 85%, enabling daily executive visibility"

Same work. Different story. Different outcome.

2. THE VISIBILITY PYRAMID

Being good is Level 1.
Being known for being good is Level 4.

I made sure my skip-level (my boss's boss) knew my name. How? Volunteered for the demo. Sent the summary email that got forwarded up.

If your boss gets hit by a bus, does the VP know your name?
If not, you're invisible.

3. THE TROJAN HORSE

I wanted to pivot from Mechanical to Software. But I had zero software experience.

So I didn't ASK for a software project. I TOOK a Mechanical problem and solved it with Software.

I automated my own job. Then showed the software team what I built.

Old Problem + New Tool = Pivot Portfolio.

4. THE SERVICE-BASED ASK

When I wanted promotion, I never said "I want more money."

I said: "I feel I've mastered my current scope. I'm looking for more responsibility so I can solve [Bigger Problem] for the team. What does the gap look like?"

This shifts the conversation from GREED to GROWTH.

THE RESULTS

4 Promotions in 7 years (IC to Leadership)
3 Major Pivots (Civil to Mech to SW to Leader)
Currently impacting 2,000+ engineers
Delivered $1B+ in programs to market

THE LESSON

Your career is an engineering problem.

Most people hope and wait.
Engineers identify variables and optimize.

I built a system. It worked for me.
Now I teach it to other engineers.
