The biggest lie in the 2025 job market? Job titles. Companies are inflating titles everywhere. What used to be "Engineer II" is now "Senior Engineer." What used to be "entry-level" has quietly disappeared from job boards. And if you're searching for "Junior Engineer" or "Associate Engineer" on LinkedIn ‚Äî you're finding almost nothing. Not because the work doesn't exist. Because the title doesn't. Here's what's actually happening: ‚Üí "Senior" is being added to mid-level postings (up ~40%). Same work. Bigger title. Makes it look like there are no jobs for anyone below senior level. ‚Üí Fewer postings require a bachelor's degree (down ~20% since 2020). Skills-based hiring is growing ‚Äî but slowly. ‚Üí "Preferred" experience has become "required" experience. That 3-5 year "preference" is now a hard filter in applicant tracking systems. ‚Üí True entry-level roles are vanishing. Companies are either eliminating them, outsourcing them, or rebranding them to justify higher experience requirements. What does this mean for you? 1. Stop filtering by title. Search by skills, responsibilities, and technologies instead. That "Senior QA Engineer" posting might actually be doing the same work a QA Engineer II would have done 3 years ago. 2. Apply up. If the actual responsibilities match your skills and experience, the title shouldn't scare you off. Companies are inflating titles ‚Äî you should inflate your applications. 3. Frame education as experience. Your senior design project, internship, co-op, or research work is real experience. Frame it that way on your resume. 4. Consider apprenticeships. Some of the best companies are building apprenticeship programs specifically because they know degree requirements are outdated. These are legitimate paths. 5. Watch the actual requirements, not the title. Read the full posting. If you hit 60-70% of the requirements, apply. The ladder didn't disappear. They just moved the labels around.
