This avatar has a proper emotional spine:

“I’m good. I’m useful. I’m trusted. So why does everyone else seem more promotable than me?”

That’s the wound.

Using Taki’s Hot Button Map, this is how I’d clean it up.

| Quadrant | Raw signal | Polished hot-button copy |
|---|---|---|
| Frustrations | Good work isn’t getting credited. Critical work only gets noticed when it breaks. No visibility outside the team. Manager’s manager doesn’t know them. Promotion feedback is vague. Self-advocacy feels gross. | “I’m doing work that matters, but somehow the credit doesn’t follow me. My manager says I’m doing well, but nobody with promotion influence seems to know what I’ve actually done.” |
| Fears | Branded as the X person. Stuck in the same role. Seen as unpromotable. Passed by newer engineers. Miss salary jumps. Become bitter. Have to leave to grow. | “I’m scared I’ll become the dependable engineer everyone trusts, but nobody promotes.” |
| Wants | Recognition. Real promotion roadmap. Higher-visibility work. Manager advocacy. Clear gap feedback. Better relationships with senior people. Chances to present and be seen. | “I want to know exactly what I need to do next so my promotion doesn’t depend on hoping someone notices.” |
| Aspirations | $10k–$30k income jump. Respect. Validation. Leadership signal. Bigger projects. More ownership. Reputation. Influence. Mentoring newer engineers. | “I want to be seen as the engineer who’s already operating at the next level, with the title, income, and reputation to match.” |

The cleanest positioning angle:

“Stop being the invisible high-performer. Build the visibility, promotion case, and leadership signal that make senior people say, ‘Yep, they’re ready.’”

That’s the centre of the dartboard.

Post hooks:

1. You’re not stuck because your code is bad. You’re stuck because your impact is invisible.

2. If your manager keeps saying “you’re doing great” but you’re still not getting promoted, you don’t have a performance problem. You have a promotion-case problem.

3. The most dangerous career label in engineering isn’t “bad performer.” It’s “reliable person we can keep exactly where they are.”

4. If senior leaders only hear your name when something breaks, your promotion case is already in trouble.

5. Good engineers think their work will speak for itself. Promoted engineers make sure the right people understand what the work actually changed.

Story post intro:

You shipped the thing.

It worked. The system stayed stable. The team moved faster. Nobody panicked. Lovely.

Then promotion season comes around and somehow the story becomes, “You’re doing great, but we need to see more leadership.”

Tiny punch in the throat, that one.

The problem isn’t that your work has no impact. It’s that your impact is trapped inside tickets, Slack threads, and vague memories your manager may or may not bring up in the room you’re not invited to.

Good work matters.

But invisible good work doesn’t build a promotion case.

Where to use each quadrant:

Frustrations are best for short-form posts, email openers, sales page intros, and “that’s me” ads. These are the daily irritations. The review cycle nonsense. The invisible project work. The manager fog. Use these to get attention fast.

Fears belong in deeper posts, webinar sections, sales conversations, and mid-page copy. Don’t overcook them. Engineers don’t want melodrama. But the fear of becoming “reliable but not promotable” is very real. Use that.

Wants are for offer copy. Program outcomes. Module names. Call-to-action sections. This is where you make the offer feel practical: promotion roadmap, manager scripts, visibility plan, impact rewrite, senior-leader relationship map.

Aspirations go near the close. They give the work meaning. More money, yes. But the bigger win is identity: “I’m not just a useful engineer anymore. I’m seen as someone with leadership weight.”

Best copy angle to test first:

“You don’t need to become louder. You need to make your impact easier to see.”

That’s strong because it removes the sleaze objection.

This avatar does not want to become the annoying self-promoter in the standup with a personal brand and a ring light. Spare us all.

They want a clean way to show the truth:

“I did valuable work. It mattered. And I’m ready for more.”

That’s your message.
