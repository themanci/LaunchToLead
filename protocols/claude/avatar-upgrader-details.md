Locked. The Invisible Performer is the right working name.

It has tension.

They’re not incompetent. They’re not lazy. They’re not getting roasted in reviews.

They’re good, but not obvious.

That’s the whole problem.

Upgraded Avatar Blueprint

Nickname: The Invisible Performer

Stage of career:  
2–5 years into engineering. Solid technically. Good reviews. Trusted to deliver, but not yet seen as a promotion-ready leader.

They’re past “new grad panic,” but not yet playing the promotion game well.

Top 3 pain points:

1. “My manager says I’m doing well, but nothing is changing.”
2. “I deliver good work, but I don’t stand out.”
3. “I don’t know how to advocate for myself without sounding cringe.”

The deeper pain is quieter:

They’re starting to wonder if being good at the job isn’t enough.

Correct. Annoying, but correct.

What they want:  
They want to be seen as ready for the next level.

Not just “liked.”  
Not just “reliable.”  
Not just “nice engineer who closes tickets.”

They want their manager and senior people to think:

“Yep. This person is operating at the next level already.”

They want a clear promotion case, better visibility, stronger relationships, and the confidence to talk about their impact without feeling like they’re selling essential oils in a team meeting.

Buying behaviour:  
They’ll pay if the offer feels practical, direct, and tied to career ROI.

A $2k–$3k program makes sense if they believe it helps them win a $15k–$30k promotion, avoid another wasted review cycle, and stop guessing what “show more leadership” means.

They are not buying motivation.

They are buying clarity, positioning, and a plan.

Inside the program:  
Good fit clients will:

- implement quickly
- bring real examples from work
- practise manager conversations
- rewrite how they describe their impact
- ask sharp questions
- take feedback without needing a cuddle and a biscuit every time

They’ll need some confidence-building, yes. But the main work is not emotional rescue.

It’s helping them see the hidden rules and act on them.

Language they use:

- “I’m doing good work, but I’m not getting promoted.”
- “My reviews are good, so I don’t know what I’m missing.”
- “I think my impact is hidden.”
- “I don’t want to brag.”
- “I thought if I worked hard enough, the promotion would happen.”
- “I don’t know how to build relationships without it feeling fake.”
- “I’m not seen as a leader yet.”
- “I’m close. I just need to know what to actually do.”

That last one is a very buying-ready sentence.

Use it.

Your clean avatar statement:

“I help early-career engineers who are doing good technical work but still feel invisible turn their impact into a clear promotion case.”

A sharper version:

“I help 2–5 year engineers stop being the invisible high-performer and build the visibility, self-advocacy, and leadership signal they need to get promoted.”
