### Method 4: The Alignment Signalâ„¢

**The Problem:** Some employees publicly bash company leadership, goals, or initiatives in circles where it can get back to leadership.

**The Rule:** You cannot be perceived as someone who isn't bought into company direction. Even if you have concerns, there's a time and place.

**The Tactic:**
- In public forums (meetings, Slack, town halls), demonstrate genuine engagement with company initiatives
- Ask clarifying questions instead of dismissive ones
- Save critical feedback for appropriate 1-on-1 channels with your direct manager
- If you disagree with a direction, frame it as "help me understand" not "this is wrong"

**The Risk:** Word travels. If you're known as someone who bashes leadership, that reputation will reach the people who decide promotions. You'll be seen as not a team player, immature, or someone who can't be trusted with more responsibility.

**The Balance:** This doesn't mean being a yes-man. Thoughtful pushback in appropriate settings shows maturity. Public cynicism or constant negativity shows the opposite.

## 2.1 The Role Saturation Thresholdâ„¢ (The 85% Rule)

### The Distinction from Phase 1

**Phase 1 Level 1 (Green Light Auditâ„¢)** asks: "Am I performing well? Am I self-sufficient? Am I helping others?"

**Phase 2 (Role Saturation Thresholdâ„¢)** asks: "Have I extracted 85% of what this role has to offer in terms of learning, influence, impact, and improvement?"

### The Problem: Premature Asking

Some employees ask for promotion simply because:
- They've been in the role for 12 months
- They're bored and want something new
- They see peers getting promoted
- They just want more money/title

This signals immaturity and that you're focused on climbing, not adding value.

### The 85% Threshold

**The Question:** Have you tapped out your role in terms of what you can learn, influence, impact, and improve?

**Signs You're NOT at 85%:**
- There's still plenty you could do to improve your area
- You haven't mastered core aspects of the role
- You're still learning fundamental skills
- There are obvious improvements you haven't made yet
- You'd be leaving significant value on the table

**Signs You ARE at 85%:**
- You've absorbed, mastered, and benefited from most of what the role offers
- Continued growth will be slow, diminishing returns
- You're hungry for more and this role is "running dry"
- You've made the major improvements available to you
- You're ready to drink from a new firehose

### The Mindset

If you're truly focused on adding value and learning as much as you can from each role, why would you move on when you haven't absorbed 85% of what it has to offer?

After 85%, it's diminishing returnsâ€”this is the right time to start thinking about the move.

## 2.4 The Gap Analysis Contractâ„¢

### The Concept

### The Approach: Collaborative, Not Pushy

### Tracking the Agreement

**The Key:** Get agreement on the list with your manager. Have them detail what they need to see from you to view you at the next level.

- Bring up "promotion" every month

**What TO Do:**
- Update them on initiative progress
- Wait until the list is mostly complete before raising promotion again

The moment you say "I'm just an X," you've defeated yourself before the race starts.

### The Paradox

**The BEST way to prove you're ready for a promotion is to do the work at the next level BEFORE you have the title.**

If you limit yourself with "I'm just an IC," you can never do this by definition. You've eliminated your path to proving you're ready.

### The Opportunity

I have led meetings, initiatives, and task forces for things I "shouldn't" have had to own. No one was stepping up, so I stepped up.

**The result:**
- Helped me get promotions
- Rave reviews from VP and Director-level leaders
- Recognition and visibility I couldn't have gotten otherwise

### The Rule

Never limit yourself with "I'm just an X." You've given up before starting.

## 6.7 The "Companies Are Evil" Cynicismâ„¢

### The Belief

"Companies only care about money. We're all just cogs in the wheel. They don't care about us."

### The Damage

If you let this mentality seep in, you will never climb.

**Two reasons:**

1. **Externally:** People won't promote someone who trashes the company. You'll be seen as a naysayer, not a leader.

2. **Internally:** You won't give 100% to a company you think is just a shill. Your performance will suffer.

### The Mature Reframe

Yes, companies care about profits. That's why they exist. **This is a good thing.**

A company that doesn't pursue profits goes out of business. Then nobody has a job.

### The Mindset Shift

Decouple the belief that a company should be a family. Companies should be:
- Moral
- Ethical
- Fair

But a company's goal is to maximize value for shareholders. **That's not evilâ€”it's the point of a company.**

The sooner you come to grips with this and make peace with it, the faster you'll succeed.

## 6.8 The Victim Mentality Trapâ„¢

### The Pattern

- "I applied 3 times and didn't get it"
- "My manager doesn't like me"
- "The cards are stacked against me"
- "It's not fair"

### The Guarantee

**When you give up and play the victim, you guarantee you won't get the promotion.**

### My Story

It took me **4 different applications and interview processes** to get my senior engineer role. Four.

If I had given up after the first, second, or third attempt, I wouldn't have gotten there.

### The Fuel Conversion

Every failed attempt is:
- Fuel for the next attempt
- A learning opportunity
- Data on what to improve
- Refinement for your next try

### The Fighter's Response

Even if:
- Your manager doesn't like you
- People seem against you
- Things feel unfair

**So what?**

Your options:
1. **Give up** â†’ Get nowhere (guaranteed)
2. **Keep fighting** â†’ Chance to overcome

Work harder. Prepare more. Prep better. Deliver more results. Add more value. **Make it undeniable that you deserve the promotion.**

### The Exception

If you're being harassed, bullied, or singled out unfairly, that's an HR issue. Handle it through proper channels.

### The Rule

Abandon victim mentality. Convert everything that would normally feed victimhood into fuel and learning opportunities.

**Always keep fighting.**

## 6.9 The Ownership Mentalityâ„¢

### The Principle

Take on as much responsibility and ownership as your manager will allow.

### The Truth About Managers

**Your manager doesn't care about your title.** If they feel you can handle it, they will give you more responsibility.

All you have to do is ask.

### What to Ask For

- "Can I lead our standup?"
- "Can I run our team sync meeting?"
- "Is there something I can take off your plate?"
- "Can I own training and onboarding new team members?"
- "Can I coach the interns?"

**There is SO much room** to take on more responsibility without a title change.

### The Manager's Perspective

Every manager is looking for their team to:
- Do more
- Produce more
- With the same resources or less

If you're asking for responsibility, taking things on, and performing wellâ€”**you're a high performer primed for promotion.**

### The Mindset

Don't wait for responsibility to be given. Ask for it. Seek it. Absorb it.

## 6.10 The Value-First Mentalityâ„¢

### The Principle

Always add value first. Before asking for anything in return.

### The Paradox

If you add value consistently, the return comes by itselfâ€”**even without asking.**

Sometimes even against your will.

### The Focus

Start thinking: "What value can I add? How can I help?"

### The Trust

The return WILL come. Focus on value creation, and recognition follows.

## 6.11 The Long-Game Mentalityâ„¢

### The Trade-Off

But when you've gotten 2-3 promotions in 10 years, and you:
- Make 40-50% more money
- Have more responsibility
- Have more influence
- Can impact more things

**Will it be worth it?** Yes. If that's what you want.

### The Human Superpower

What makes humans unique is our ability to **pay a price today for a reward tomorrow.**

Don't get short-sighted. Don't expect immediate returnsâ€”in a day, a week, or a month.

### The Bank Account

Every investment you make in yourself and your career goes into a bank account.

**It's in the bank.** It's only a matter of time until you can withdraw it.

Maybe it's with this manager. Maybe another department. Maybe another company.

But it's in the bank. Don't forget that.

## 6.12 The Relationship Investment Mentalityâ„¢

### The Seed Metaphor

View relationships as planting seeds.

**Different seeds produce fruit at different times:**
- Some in 2 months
- Some in 2 years
- Some in 20 years

Imagine you have a bag of mixed seeds and you don't know which one will produce fruit when.

**Is the solution to plant none?**

No. You plant as many as possible.

### The Unpredictability

Some relationships will bear fruit in:
- 1 day
- 1 week
- 1 month
- 5 years
- 10 years
- 30 years

**You can't predict it.**

### The Anti-Manipulation Rule

Don't build relationships with a manipulative mindset: "I'm building this one so in 1 year I can do X."

**No.** Just build them.

Always be building. As far and wide and deep as you can.

### The Math

You never know when your paths will recross with someone. You never know when a relationship will become beneficial.

**So plant as many seeds as you can. Everywhere. All the time.**

The more you plant, the more will be available to you over your career.

## 6.13 The Feedback Satellite Mentalityâ„¢

### The Mode

Convince yourself that you are in **constant satellite receiving mode** for feedback.

Always scanning. Always open. Always processing.

### The Delusion to Abandon

Abandon any self-delusion that you are spectacular with minimal room to grow.

### The Tom Brady Principle

I once saw a video with Tom Bradyâ€”arguably the best QB who ever lived.

Mid-career. After winning multiple Super Bowls. A coach gave him a tiny, little tip.

Tom acted like he just now learned how to be a QB.

**To be the best, you have to always believe you have huge room to improve.**

### The Gratitude Shift

When someone gives you a way to improve, you should be:
- Thanking them

Not resentful. Not defensive.

### The Sensitivity Cure

Abandon sensitivity to feedback. Don't take it personally.

Just:

2. Apply what you can

### The Growth Source

We learn and grow FAR more from negative feedback than positive.

- Ask for it
- Make people know you want it
- Show you're comfortable with it

### The Side Benefit

People LOVE working with someone who:
- Has self-awareness
- Is open to critical feedback
- Can be coached and improved

They feel they can fix things with you. They feel safe working with you.

### The Principle

- Taking notes in a meeting

## PART C: THE IC TO MANAGER TRANSITION

The fundamental shifts required when moving from Individual Contributor to Manager.

---

## 6.16 The Accountability Elevationâ„¢

### The Fundamental Change

**You are now responsible and accountable to produce results.**

The buck stops with you.

### The New Reality

Telling your manager's manager "Well, we didn't get it done" is **not an acceptable answer anymore.**

This new load of responsibility and accountability is a huge bump up. It's usually eye-opening for first-time managers.

### The Shield Principle

Your team does their best work when you **shield them from pressure** and absorb it yourself.

If they're constantly stressed, they can't do their best work.

### The Thankless Nature

It's a thankless thing:
- You shield them from pressure
- You don't tell them how much you're absorbing
- They think there's no pressure
- You're quietly taking the hits so they can focus

**That's being a manager.** You take on a lot and don't get thanked for it. Sometimes no one even knows you're doing it.

Get used to it.

---

## 6.17 The Coach Transformationâ„¢

### The Fundamental Shift

You are no longer a first-line, hands-on doer.

**You're going from star player to coach.**

### The Mental Shift

You can't touch everything and do everything anymore. You are a coach.

Focus on **enabling others to do**, not micromanaging and doing the doing yourself.

### The Common Mistake

Most engineers take a while to figure this out when they become managers.

They keep trying to be the star player AND the coach. It doesn't work.

### The Outcome-First Approach

Ask your team to deliver what you WANT, but give them freedom on the HOW.

**Good:**
> "I need to be able to see progress on projects every day to know where we are."

Let them figure out how.

**Bad:**
> "I want you to make a dashboard that looks like this, using this tool, this color, these fields..."

### The Trust Requirement

The coach never subs in for the quarterback or running back in football.

If you don't trust the team to do what they need to do, it won't work. You can't do it for them.

**Better to let them fumble around a little and learn than to do it yourself.**

Know your role. Stick to it.

---

## 6.18 The Metrics Mindsetâ„¢

### The New Requirement

You need to care about metrics a lot more as a manager.

**You must have a way to measure:**
- Is the team delivering results?
- Are we on track?
- Are we failing?
- Are we behind?

### The Danger of Gut Feel

You can't leave this to:
- Gut feel
- Casual comments in standups
- Vibes from sync meetings

You need to understand which metrics truly gauge success or failureâ€”then track them closely.

### The Initial Resistance

The team won't like this at first. It feels like micromanagement.

### The Eventual Appreciation

When metrics get the team on track and delivering well, they start to appreciate them.

**Why?** Because metrics become objective truth and evidence that they're doing their jobs well.

Metrics shift from "surveillance" to "validation."

**That's a great place to be.**

---

## 6.19 The Relationship Recalibrationâ„¢

### The Peer-to-Manager Dynamic

If you manage former peers, this is tricky to navigate.

**Handle it with:**
- Humility
- Grace
- Empathy
- BUT still authority

You ARE the manager. You ARE responsible and accountable. Don't forget that.

But be humble at the same time.

### The Complaining Rule

**One of the most off-putting things a manager can do: complain down.**

Never complain about things to people who report to you.

Never use 1-on-1s as venting sessions for yourself.

### The Perception Damage

Complaining down makes you seem:
- Amateur
- Weak
- Not a strong leader

**Complain UP. Vent UP. Never down.**

### The New Dynamic

You're a manager now. You can't interact with reports the same way you would with peers.

They need to:
- Trust you
- Believe you're a good leader
- Want to follow you into any situation
- Know you're strong, resilient, and have their back
- Know you'll go to bat for them

**Don't do things that make them question their faith in you.**

---

## 6.20 The Time Reallocation Protocolâ„¢

### Where Manager Time Goes

A new manager's time should go into:

1. **Enabling the team to succeed**
2. **Removing roadblocks**
3. **Ensuring people have the skills they need**
4. **Training when skills are missing**
5. **Building processes and practices for team success**

### The Reminder

**You are the coach, not the star player.**

Put your time into making the team function, operate, and execute better.

### The Trust Imperative

Trust the team to do the work.

The coach never subs in for the running back. If you don't trust the team, it won't work. You can't do it for them.

Better to let them fumble and learn than to do it yourself.

**Know your role. Stick to it.**
