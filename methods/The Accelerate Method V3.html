‚Ä∫ Organic Broadcasting
You don't brag; you "update." Strategic visibility without self-promotion.

Tactics

  • Volunteer to present at team meetings that include skip-level leadership
  • Volunteer to give demos to stakeholders (sprint reviews, product demos)
  • Write summary emails that naturally get forwarded up the chain

‚Ä∫ The Echo Email
Summary email structure designed to be forwarded up the chain‚Äîor create visibility directly.

Core Principles

  1. Keep it SHORT. Managers get tons of emails. Don't make it long.
  2. Share credit with others. Best visibility comes from recognizing others while being part of the story.
  3. Don't brag directly. Never self-praise. Get credit through giving credit.

When to use: After completing something notable with teammates.

Why it works: You're sharing credit, but you're obviously part of the win.

Template 2: The Value-Add Tool (To Your Manager)

When to use: When you've built something useful beyond normal scope.

"Hey [Manager], Just wanted to share that I built a new tool that can automate pulling test case reports. Thought it might be useful for tracking progress. I can set up a quick 1-on-1 to go over it if interested."
Template 3: The Big Team Recognition (To Skip-Level, Wide Distribution)

Why it works: You're assumed part of the team (credit) + you're the one recognizing everyone (leadership credit). Double win.

‚Ä∫ The Alignment Signal
You cannot be perceived as someone who isn't bought into company direction.

The Tactic

  • In public forums (meetings, Slack, town halls), demonstrate genuine engagement with company initiatives
  • Ask clarifying questions instead of dismissive ones
  • Save critical feedback for appropriate 1-on-1 channels with your direct manager
  • If you disagree with a direction, frame it as "help me understand" not "this is wrong"
The Risk: Word travels. If you're known as someone who bashes leadership, that reputation will reach the people who decide promotions. You'll be seen as not a team player, immature, or untrustworthy with more responsibility.

The Balance: This doesn't mean being a yes-man. Thoughtful pushback in appropriate settings shows maturity. Public cynicism or constant negativity shows the opposite.

‚Ä∫ The Gap Pioneer Framework
You're not just an order-taker executing commands. You're a builder, a pioneer who sees what the company needs before anyone asks.

Step 1: Identify Gaps Worth Proposing

Good gaps to propose:

  • Everyone knows it's a problem

As workload grows, discuss with manager/skip about formalizing it. Could become a promotion for you, or a new role.

The Win-Win: Even if you don't get the role you created, you've established yourself as a visionary who sees what the company needs, someone who builds not just executes, a leader before you have the title. This reputation leads to promotion anyway.

PHASE 2

The Promotion Protocol

10 Methods

We teach clients that a promotion is not something you "get"; it is a new contract you negotiate by offering more value.

‚Ä∫ The Role Saturation Threshold (85% Rule)
The Question: Have you extracted 85% of what this role offers in terms of learning, influence, impact, and improvement?

The Distinction from Phase 1

Phase 1 (Green Light Audit‚Ñ¢) asks: "Am I performing well? Am I self-sufficient? Am I helping others?"

Phase 2 (Role Saturation) asks: "Have I extracted 85% of what this role has to offer?"

Signs You're NOT at 85%

  • Still plenty you could do to improve your area
  • Haven't mastered core aspects of the role
  • Still learning fundamental skills
  • Obvious improvements you haven't made yet

Signs You ARE at 85%

  • Absorbed, mastered, and benefited from most of what role offers
  • Continued growth will be slow, diminishing returns
  • Hungry for more and role is "running dry"
  • Made the major improvements available to you
The Mindset: If you're truly focused on adding value and learning, why would you move on when you haven't absorbed 85%? After 85%, it's diminishing returns‚Äîthis is the right time to start thinking about the move.
‚Ä∫ The Optimal Timing Strategy

Best Time: Annual Review (with "Exceeds Expectations")

  • Your manager has already admitted you're performing at a high level
  • You likely have evidence of hitting the 85% threshold
  • Role, performance, future, and potential are already on the table
  • It's the most appropriate venue for this discussion

The One-Month Notice Rule

Give your manager at least one month notice that you want to discuss your future in an upcoming 1-on-1.

  • Not a good topic to spring with no warning
  • Gives manager time to think, gather feedback, prepare
  • Shows forethought and maturity on your part
  • Signals you want serious feedback and discussion
What NOT to do: Bring it up casually in passing ‚Ä¢ Spring it unexpectedly ‚Ä¢ Mention it repeatedly as a sidenote (becomes unserious)
‚Ä∫ The Evidence Arsenal
Come prepared with documented proof of your value.

What to Bring

  1. 360 Feedback ‚Äî Positive feedback from peers, seniors, direct reports that speaks well of you
  2. Performance Reviews ‚Äî Your yearly reviews showing strong performance
  3. Accomplishments List ‚Äî Documented impacts you've had, problems solved, value added
  4. Role Saturation Evidence ‚Äî Be prepared to explain why/how the role doesn't have much more juice to squeeze
The Frame: You're not just asking for a promotion‚Äîyou're demonstrating that you're hungry and eager to drink from the firehose, and this one is running dry.
‚Ä∫ The Service-Based Script Library
Frame it as a desire for more responsibility and impact‚Äînot just wanting a title. Ban the word "Promotion" from the initial conversation.

Bad Approaches (The "Greed Pitch")

  • "I want more money"
  • "I want a promotion"
  • "I've been here X years, I deserve this"
  • "My peers got promoted, why not me?"
The Core Script
"I feel I've mastered my current scope. I'm looking for more responsibility so I can solve [Bigger Problem] for the team. What does the gap between my current output and that level look like?"
Annual Review Context
"Thank you for the feedback on my performance this year. Based on what we've discussed, I feel I've gotten to a place where I've absorbed most of what this role has to offer. I'm hungry to take on more responsibility and continue growing. Can we talk about what the path to the next level looks like?"

Guiding Principles for All Scripts

  1. Respectful ‚Äî Always professional, never confrontational
  2. Empathetic ‚Äî Acknowledge their position and constraints
  3. Professional ‚Äî Keep emotions in check
  4. Fact-Seeking ‚Äî Push for tangible, actionable feedback
  5. Accountability-Creating ‚Äî Make it hard for them to give vague, non-actionable responses
‚Ä∫ The Objection Playbook
Objection 1: "We're not doing promotions right now"
"I understand there are constraints right now. Can you give me a date or timeframe when we can revisit this after those constraints are removed?"

Why it works: Holds them accountable, prevents indefinite delays, gives you a checkpoint.

Objection 2: "You're doing great, keep it up"
"Thanks for the positive feedback! Can you be more specific about what I'm doing great at? And what other things would I need to be doing great at 6 months from now to have this discussion again?"

Why it works: Converts vague praise into actionable items, forces specificity.

Objection 3: "You're not ready yet"
"I appreciate the honesty. Can we go into more detail about what would make me ready? What specific things can I work on and improve over the next 6-12 months so that I would be ready?"

Follow-up: Get specifics documented, set a date for next check-in, provide monthly updates on progress.

Objection 4: "I don't control that, it's HR/leadership"
"Good to know. If I pursue an opportunity with HR or leadership, do I have your support? If they reached out to you about your opinion, would you be in my corner?"

If Yes: Great, you have an ally. If No/Hesitant: Get specifics on what would earn their support.

Objection 5: "Let's revisit this at annual review"
"Sounds good. Can you give me specifics I can work on between now and then? I'd like to have a picture of what I'd need to accomplish so I know I'm working towards tangible, achievable goals."

‚Ä∫ The Promotion Positioning Philosophy
The promotion conversation shouldn't be one giant, high-stakes conversation.

We are NOT pursuing: Some salesman tactic where you play 3D chess with your manager to convince them to say "yes" in a single moment.

We ARE pursuing: Slowly painting yourself as the obvious candidate. Gradually tipping the balance in your favor. Building so much evidence that people feel dumb for even questioning whether you deserve a promotion.

Your goal is to build a body of work and evidence that becomes so overwhelming it's the 600-pound gorilla in the room. It can't be ignored. If it IS ignored, it's just ridiculous.

A private resource YOU keep to build your case‚Äîevidence of what you've done, impact, value added, feedback received, metrics improved, problems solved.

How to Use It

‚Ä∫ The Compensation Negotiation at Promotion

The Empowerment Principle

By getting the offer, you are empowered. They chose YOU. You are the candidate they want. By default, you have leverage. If they want you, you have something of value. Don't sell yourself short.

The Fear to Abandon

The Preparation

  1. Get data from other companies about what that role/level is worth
  2. Ask colleagues if they're willing to share
  3. Use open-source salary data (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind, etc.)
  4. Do your homework before the conversation
The Tactic: Go big first. Negotiate down. If you ask for less than you want, you leave money on the table. Start high, be willing to meet in the middle.

‚Ä∫ The "Not My Job" Funeral
Eliminate "That's not my job" from your vocabulary. Replace with "The Team Success Metric" ‚Äî If the team fails, you fail. If the team succeeds, you have a claim to that success.

‚Ä∫ The Opportunity Filter
Not all extra work is created equal. Some opportunities accelerate your career. Others bury you with no credit.

Before Taking On Extra Work, Ask:

  1. Is this value-added work? ‚Äî Is it tracked in your team's backlog? Is it an actual priority? Or is it someone's pet project?
  2. Will this be visible? ‚Äî Can you make your manager aware? Will there be opportunities for updates and report-outs? Or will it disappear into a black hole?
  3. Can you actually deliver? ‚Äî Do you have capacity to do this well? Or will it cause core work to slip?

  • üö© Project ongoing for a long time with no progress ‚Äî Signals no one cares or expects delivery
  • üö© Project with unclear or low value ‚Äî You'll put in effort with little payoff
  • üö© Manager's pet project with no organizational buy-in ‚Äî You become attached to something that may never matter
The Rule: Don't be the first to volunteer for low-value work. Let someone else get bogged down by it.
‚Ä∫ The Quality Firewall
"Core First, Expansion Second." Only take on extra scope if your core performance remains at an "A" level.

The Stop Sign

A drop in core performance is your signal to immediately drop extra scope.

The Career Death Spiral: Being a "weak performer with a lot of tasks" is worse than being a solid performer with normal scope. This kills careers.
‚Ä∫ The Early Warning System
Don't wait until things slip. Recognize warning signs early.

Warning Signs to Watch For

  • You're cutting corners on core deliverables
  • You're missing small deadlines or commitments
  • Quality of your work is declining
  • You're feeling overwhelmed or stressed
  • You're not preparing adequately for meetings
When you see these signs: It's time to have a conversation BEFORE things actually slip.
‚Ä∫ The Bad News Early Protocol
A hallmark of promotion-ready engineers: Delivering bad news fast and early.

Telling your manager as soon as you think a project is at risk is a GOOD thing. It signals you can identify risk early and adjust.

If this makes your manager mad: You have a bad manager. Good managers appreciate early warning.

How to Deliver Bad News

  1. Come prepared with context:
    "This is taking more than expected. Scope has widened. We've hit some snags causing delays. It's clear we need more resources."
  2. Come with proposed solutions (never just problems):
    "I think if I get help from [person], or if we reprioritize these 3 projects, or if we extend this deadline, we can still deliver."
Never say: "We can't deliver" and stop there. Always say: "We can't deliver, BUT here are some options..."
‚Ä∫ The Collaborative Problem-Solving Approach
When you deliver bad news with proposed solutions, you're now collaborating with your manager to solve a problem you didn't directly cause.

This Leads To:

  • Manager helping you reprioritize
  • Manager getting you more resources
  • Manager extending deadlines on other things
  • A healthy, synced-up relationship

No risk of "burning bridges" because you're working together, not dumping problems on them.

Prerequisites

  • Good, healthy relationship with manager
  • Regular status reports and checkpoints
  • Maturity to deliver bad news early
  • Always bringing solutions with problems

When you volunteer to help outside teams, you MUST:

  • Follow through to completion
  • Not disappear halfway or fizzle out
The Risk: Your external-facing reputation becomes "he starts but doesn't finish." This is career poison.
‚Ä∫ The Support Role Entry
When you step into someone else's project or area, you risk stepping on toes, being seen as a glory-seeker, or making people defensive. Always Enter as Support.

  2. Later: Others say "No, it's okay, we really want your input."
  3. Finally: You can weigh in with a still-humble attitude, adding value without stepping on toes.

‚Ä∫ The Strategic No

  2. Test the priority: "Do you think this is more value-added and higher priority than everything I currently have on my plate?"
  3. Suggest a delay: "Could we delay this until I'm free so I can work on it properly?"

üå± The Seed Metaphor: View relationships as planting seeds. Different seeds produce fruit at different times: Some in 2 months, some in 2 years, some in 20 years.

‚ùì The Question: Imagine you have a bag of mixed seeds and you don't know which one will produce fruit when. Is the solution to plant none? No. You plant as many as possible.

‚ùå The Anti-Manipulation Rule: Don't build relationships with a manipulative mindset: "I'm building this one so in 1 year I can do X." No. Just build them. Always be building. As far and wide and deep as you can.
üí° The Math: Plant as many seeds as you can. Everywhere. All the time. The more you plant, the more will be available to you over your career.

üôè The Gratitude Shift: When someone gives you a way to improve, you should be thanking them, not resentful or defensive.

‚úÖ The Process:

  1. Internalize the feedback
  2. Apply what you can
  3. Move on
üí° Side Benefit: People LOVE working with someone who has self-awareness, is open to critical feedback, and can be coached.

üîë Two Critical Mindsets:
  1. Never think you're the smartest person in the room. Always assume you're the dumbest person there. By default, you now have something to learn from every other person.
  2. Stop being afraid to look dumb. If you're afraid to look dumb, you'll never make mistakes. If you can't make mistakes, you can't grow.

üìä The Math: It's better to look dumb for 2 months, then pass everyone... than to try to "never look dumb" and stay dumb forever.

üí° Release: Let go of ego protection. Embrace looking foolish temporarily. That's the price of growth.

‚Ä∫ 6.16 The Accountability Elevation
üîÑ The Fundamental Change: You are now responsible and accountable to produce results. The buck stops with you. Telling your manager's manager "Well, we didn't get it done" is not an acceptable answer anymore.

üõ°Ô∏è The Shield Principle: Your team does their best work when you shield them from pressure and absorb it yourself. If they're constantly stressed, they can't do their best work.

üí≠ The Thankless Nature: You shield them from pressure. You don't tell them how much you're absorbing. They think there's no pressure. You're quietly taking the hits so they can focus. That's being a manager. Get used to it.
‚Ä∫ 6.17 The Coach Transformation
üîÑ The Fundamental Shift: You are no longer a first-line, hands-on doer. You're going from star player to coach.

üß† The Mental Shift: You can't touch everything and do everything anymore. Focus on enabling others to do, not micromanaging and doing the doing yourself.

‚Ä∫ 6.18 The Metrics Mindset
üìã The New Requirement: You need to care about metrics a lot more as a manager. You must have a way to measure:
  • Is the team delivering results?
  • Are we on track?
  • Are we failing? Are we behind?
‚ö†Ô∏è The Danger of Gut Feel: You can't leave this to gut feel, casual comments in standups, or vibes from sync meetings. You need to understand which metrics truly gauge success‚Äîthen track them closely.

üîÑ The Evolution:

  • Initial resistance: The team won't like metrics at first. It feels like micromanagement.
  • Eventual appreciation: When metrics get the team on track and delivering well, they start to appreciate them. Metrics shift from "surveillance" to "validation."

‚öñÔ∏è The Peer-to-Manager Dynamic: If you manage former peers, this is tricky. Handle it with: Humility, Grace, Empathy‚ÄîBUT still authority. You ARE the manager. You ARE responsible and accountable. Don't forget that.
üö´ The Complaining Rule: One of the most off-putting things a manager can do: complain down.
  • Never complain about things to people who report to you
  • Never use 1-on-1s as venting sessions for yourself

Complaining down makes you seem amateur, weak, not a strong leader. Complain UP. Vent UP. Never down.

üéØ The New Dynamic: Your reports need to: Trust you, believe you're a good leader, want to follow you into any situation, know you're strong and resilient, know you'll go to bat for them. Don't do things that make them question their faith in you.
‚Ä∫ 6.20 The Time Reallocation Protocol
üìã Where Manager Time Goes: A new manager's time should go into:
  1. Enabling the team to succeed
  2. Removing roadblocks
  3. Ensuring people have the skills they need
  4. Training when skills are missing
  5. Building processes and practices for team success

üéØ The Reminder: You are the coach, not the star player. Put your time into making the team function, operate, and execute better.
