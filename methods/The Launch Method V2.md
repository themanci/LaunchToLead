# The Launch Method V2: Complete Methodology

> **Target Audience:** College students, recent graduates, and early-career engineers seeking their first engineering role.

---

## Overview

The Launch Method is a comprehensive framework for landing your first engineering job. It covers everything from resume writing to interview performance, with specific tactics proven to work in the modern job market.

**Core Philosophy:** Hard work is the prerequisite. Strategy is the multiplier.

---

# PHASE 1: THE FOUNDATION

## 1.1 The "Me-in-We" Extraction

### The Problem
Most students and juniors list "Team Capstone Project" and describe what the team built. They hide behind "we" language.

### The Method
Force a distinction between the project's success and YOUR contribution.

### The Tactic
Strip away the "We." Even if it was a team effort, every resume bullet point must describe:
- The specific gear YOU designed
- The code YOU committed
- The analysis YOU ran
- The decision YOU influenced

### Example Transformation
| Before (Team Language) | After (Me-in-We Extraction) |
|------------------------|----------------------------|
| "We developed a mobile app for campus navigation" | "Designed and implemented the GPS tracking module using Swift, reducing location accuracy errors by 40%" |

---

## 1.2 The "Passion Proof" Indicator

### The Problem
Candidates list required coursework and mandatory projects. This shows minimum compliance, not genuine interest.

### The Method
Highlight voluntary work that wasn't required—this is "skin in the game."

### The Tactic
Ask yourself: "What did I do that I didn't HAVE to do?"

**Examples of Passion Proof:**
- Side projects outside of class
- Voluntary research with a professor
- Self-taught skills (certifications, online courses)
- Student organizations or competition teams
- Open source contributions
- Personal blog or portfolio

### What If You Don't Have Passion Proof?
If you're lacking here, create some:
- **30-Day Sprint:** Pick one skill relevant to your target role and build a small project
- **Document Everything:** Even small experiments count if you can articulate what you learned
- **Contribute to Open Source:** Even documentation fixes show initiative

### Why It Matters
Recruiters see hundreds of resumes with identical coursework. Passion proof separates "interested enough to graduate" from "genuinely passionate about this field."

---

## 1.3 The "Universal Translator" Technique

### The Problem
Candidates get pigeonholed. A Civil Engineer can't get a Mechanical job because the recruiter doesn't understand "Highway Design."

### The Origin Story
When I applied to John Deere as a Mechanical Engineer, my only professional experience was 1 year at the Iowa Department of Transportation as a Highway Design Engineer. I realized the content didn't matter—the competency did.

### The Tactic: The Jargon Audit
Go through every bullet point on your resume. If a word is specific to your old industry, delete it and replace it with the universal engineering competency.

| Industry Jargon | Universal Competency |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| "Pavement gradation analysis" | "Data-driven material analysis" |
| "Highway alignment design" | "CAD-based geometric optimization" |
| "AASHTO compliance verification" | "Regulatory compliance documentation" |
| "Traffic impact studies" | "Stakeholder-facing technical presentations" |

### The Goal
Turn a niche candidate into a universal hire. Make it easy for ANY engineering hiring manager to understand your value.

---

## 1.4 The "Pattern Interrupt" Mindset

### The Problem
Candidates are submissive. They wait to be picked. They approach recruiters like beggars asking for a favor.

### The Origin Story
At Iowa State's Engineering career fair, after a long day of forgettable conversations, I walked up to a recruiter and said: **"If you don't give me an internship, you're making a huge mistake."**

His response? "Haha! Tell me why that is!?"

That conversation changed everything.

### The Tactic: Confidence Calibration
This isn't about being arrogant or cocky. It's about viewing yourself as an asset to be acquired, not a beggar asking for a favor.

**Mindset Shift:**
| Beggar Mindset | Asset Mindset |
|----------------|---------------|
| "I hope they pick me" | "Let me show you what I bring" |
| "I'll take anything" | "Here's why I'm a great fit for THIS role" |
| "Thank you for your time" | "I'm excited about the mutual value here" |

### Why It Works
Recruiters talk to hundreds of nervous, forgettable candidates. A confident (not arrogant) candidate who clearly articulates their value stands out immediately.

# PHASE 2: THE IMPACT EQUATION (Resume Mastery)

## 2.1 The Pyramid of Value

### The Problem
Most resumes are a "list of duties"—tasks you were assigned. This impresses no one.

### The Framework
Visualize candidate quality as a pyramid:

```
                    ▲
                   /█\        TOP TIER: Impact + Validation
                  / █ \       "Automated tracking, saving 85% time"
                 /  █  \      → RESULT: HIRED
                /   █   \
               /    █    \    MIDDLE TIER: Results
              /     █     \   "Maintained spreadsheets for 3 projects"
             /      █      \  → RESULT: CONSIDERED
            /       █       \
           /        █        \ BOTTOM TIER: Tasks
          /         █         \"Maintained spreadsheets"
         /▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀▀\→ RESULT: IGNORED
```

### Your Goal
Every bullet on your resume must be TOP TIER.

---

## 2.2 The 4-Part Bullet Formula

### The Formula
```
[Task] + [Metric] + [How] + [Why] = IMPACT
```

| Component | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Task** | What you accomplished | "Redesigned data tracking system" |
| **Metric** | Quantifiable proof | "for 3 major programs, 100 daily tests" |
| **How** | Your specific approach | "using Python script with Azure DevOps API" |
| **Why** | Business impact | "cutting maintenance time 85%, enabling daily executive reporting" |

### Complete Example
**Before (Task Only):**
> "Maintained spreadsheets for test results."

**After (Impact Equation):**
> "Solely managed data tracking for 3 major programs across a 10-person team, handling 100+ daily tests. Developed custom Python automation touching the Azure DevOps API, cutting maintenance time by 85% and enabling daily executive visibility into quality metrics."

---

## 2.3 Finding Metrics When You Don't Have Access

### The Problem
"But I don't know the business impact numbers!"

### 5 Ways to Find Hidden Metrics

1. **Ask your manager or team lead**
   - "What was the business impact of [project]?"
   - They often have data you don't.

2. **Check company dashboards/reports**
   - Quarterly reports, Jira stats, internal KPIs

3. **Calculate time savings**
   - If your script saves 30 min/day × 5 people × 250 days = 625 hours/year
   - At $50/hour loaded cost = $31,250 saved

4. **Use reasonable estimates with qualifiers**
   - "Reduced processing time by approximately 40%"
   - "Estimated $15K annual savings"

5. **Focus on scale instead of dollars**
   - "Supported 3 product lines"
   - "Used by 50+ engineers daily"
   - "Processed 10,000+ records"

## 2.5 Resume Tailoring Strategy

### The Problem
One resume does not fit all applications.

### The Method
Structure by RELEVANCE, not just recency.

### The Process

1. **Identify Target Role Requirements**
   - Job duties/responsibilities
   - "Must haves"
   - "Nice to haves"

2. **Reorder Your Experience**
   - Most relevant experience on top (even if not most recent)
   - Most relevant bullets first within each section

3. **Keyword Alignment**
   - Match language from the job posting
   - Use the same terminology they use
   - Don't invent experience, but DO translate your experience into their language

### ATS Optimization

**What is ATS?**
Applicant Tracking Systems scan resumes before humans see them. If your resume doesn't match their keywords, you're invisible.

**How to Optimize:**
1. Use AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude) to extract keywords from job postings
2. Identify recurring phrases, skills, and technologies
3. Naturally incorporate these into your resume (don't keyword stuff)
4. Use standard section headings (Education, Experience, Skills)
5. Avoid graphics, tables, or unusual formatting that ATS can't parse

**Tools to Research:**
- Look up what ATS systems companies use
- Test your resume with free ATS scanners
- Keep formatting clean and parseable

# PHASE 3: THE APPLICATION STRATEGY

## 3.1 Company Research Framework

### The Problem
Most candidates spray-and-pray: apply to 100 companies, hope for 1 callback.

### The Method
Target 10-15 companies strategically, with deep research on each.

### Research Sources
| Source | What to Look For |
|--------|------------------|
| **Company Website** | Mission, values, products, recent news, leadership |
| **Glassdoor/Indeed** | Employee reviews, interview experiences, salary data |
| **LinkedIn** | Company posts, employee content, growth trajectory |
| **News/Press** | Recent announcements, financial health, expansion |
| **Social Media** | Culture signals, employee engagement, public perception |

### What Research Gives You
1. **Better Interview Answers:** You can speak specifically about why THIS company
2. **Intelligent Questions:** Ask about recent initiatives, challenges, or strategy
3. **Red Flag Detection:** Avoid companies you wouldn't want to work for anyway
4. **Confidence:** Walk in knowing you belong there

### Red Flags to Watch For
- Consistent negative reviews about management or culture
- High turnover signals in reviews
- Recent layoffs or financial instability
- Misalignment with your values or work style

---

## 3.2 The Phone Screen

### What Phone Screens Are Looking For
Phone screens are a basic filter. The company is checking:

1. **Can you communicate clearly?** (Speak coherently, not rambling)
2. **Are you genuinely interested in THIS company?** (Not just any job)
3. **Do your skills roughly match?** (Basic qualification check)
4. **Are there deal breakers?** (Location, salary, visa, availability, start date)

### Common Phone Screen Questions
- "Tell me about yourself" (30-60 second pitch)
- "Why are you interested in [Company]?"
- "What are you looking for in your next role?"
- "Walk me through your experience with [X]"
- "What's your timeline/availability?"
- "What are your salary expectations?" (if asked)

### Red Flags to Avoid
| Red Flag | What It Signals |
|----------|-----------------|
| "I'll take anything" | Desperate, not genuinely interested |
| Unable to articulate why this company | Didn't do research, applying everywhere |
| Salary expectations wildly off | Wasted time if it's not a fit |
| Unclear communication | Will struggle in interviews and on the job |
| Badmouthing previous employers | Negative attitude, potential culture problem |

### Questions YOU Should Ask
- "What does success look like in this role in the first 6 months?"
- "What's the team structure like?"
- "What's the interview process from here?"
- "Is there anything about my background you'd like me to clarify?"

These questions also help YOU filter out bad opportunities.

---

## 3.3 Career Fair Strategy

### The Fundamentals
Career fairs are a unique opportunity for face-to-face first impressions. Most candidates waste them.

### Before the Fair
1. **Research attending companies** - Know who's coming, prioritize your targets
2. **Prepare your pitch** - 60-90 second introduction (see below)
3. **Bring tailored resumes** - If possible, versions for different roles
4. **Dress appropriately** - Business casual minimum, match the industry
5. **Prepare questions** - Show you researched the company

### The 60-90 Second Pitch Structure
```
1. HOOK (5 seconds)
   - Name + major + graduation date
   - One sentence about what makes you interesting

2. BODY (45-60 seconds)
   - 2-3 relevant experiences or skills
   - Tie them to what you know about the company
   - Use Impact Equation language

3. CLOSE (15-20 seconds)
   - Express specific interest in a role/area
   - Ask a thoughtful question
   - Request next steps
```

### Example Pitch
> "Hi, I'm [Name], a senior in Mechanical Engineering graduating in May. I spent last summer at [Company] where I redesigned a fixture assembly process that cut changeover time by 30%. I'm really interested in [Target Company]'s work in agricultural automation—I saw your recent announcement about the autonomous tractor platform. I'd love to learn more about entry-level opportunities on the design engineering team. What does a typical project look like for new engineers?"

### During the Fair
- **Be memorable, not forgettable** - Have energy, make eye contact, firm handshake
- **Ask questions** - Show genuine curiosity about the company
- **Take notes** - Write down names, follow-up items, impressions
- **Get contact info** - Ask for business cards or LinkedIn connections
- **Manage your time** - Prioritize target companies, don't spend 30 min at one booth

### After the Fair
- **Follow up within 24-48 hours** - Email or LinkedIn message
- **Reference your conversation** - "It was great discussing [topic] at the ISU career fair"
- **Reiterate interest** - Reinforce why you're excited about the opportunity
- **Include your resume** - Make it easy for them to forward you internally

### What NOT to Do
- Walk up with no research ("So what does your company do?")
- Hand over resume and walk away
- Ask about salary/benefits immediately
- Complain about job search difficulties
- Be forgettable (generic pitch, no energy, no questions)

# PHASE 4: LINKEDIN OPTIMIZATION (Quick Wins)

## 4.1 The LinkedIn Quick Wins Framework

### The Problem
Most students and early-career engineers completely neglect LinkedIn or treat it like a static resume dump.

### The Goal
Make your LinkedIn profile work for you 24/7, getting you on recruiter radars even when you're not actively applying.

### The 5 LinkedIn Pillars

---

### Pillar 1: Profile Photo & Banner
- **Photo:** Professional headshot, clear face, friendly expression
- **Banner:** Use it! Can be company-branded, industry-related, or showcase a project

---

### Pillar 2: Headline Optimization
**Default (Boring):** "Student at Iowa State University"

**Optimized:** "Mechanical Engineering Student | Passionate About Agricultural Automation | Seeking Summer 2025 Internship"

**Formula:** [Role/Major] | [Specialty/Interest] | [What You're Looking For]

---

### Pillar 3: About Section
Your About section is your 30-second pitch in written form.

**Structure:**
1. **Opening Hook** (1-2 sentences) - What drives you, what you're known for
2. **Key Strengths** (3-5 bullets) - Skills, experiences, accomplishments
3. **What You're Looking For** (1-2 sentences) - Make it easy for recruiters
4. **Call to Action** - "Let's connect" or "Reach out at [email]"

**Example:**
> I'm a Mechanical Engineering senior at Iowa State with a passion for turning complex problems into elegant solutions. After spending two summers in manufacturing environments, I've developed a deep appreciation for design-for-assembly and continuous improvement.
>
> **What I bring:**
> - SolidWorks (CSWA Certified) & ANSYS FEA experience
> - 2 internships focused on fixture design and process optimization
> - Capstone project: Redesigned assembly fixture, reducing changeover time 30%
>
> I'm seeking full-time opportunities in product design or manufacturing engineering starting May 2025.
>
> Let's connect—I'd love to learn about your team's work.

---

### Pillar 4: Experience Section
Treat this like your resume, but with more room to breathe.

**Each role should include:**
- Impact Equation bullets (same as resume)
- More context and storytelling than a resume allows
- Relevant media (project photos, presentations, documents)

---

### Pillar 5: Featured Section
This is your portfolio showcase.

**What to Feature:**
- PDF of your best resume version (targeting your dream role)
- Capstone project summary or poster
- Any published work, presentations, or certificates
- Links to portfolio, GitHub, or personal website

---

## 4.2 Strategic Posting & Engagement

### The Problem
Students think LinkedIn is just for job searching. They miss the visibility opportunity.

### The Opportunity
Recruiters don't just search for candidates—they also notice active, engaged professionals in their feed.

### Engagement Strategy (15 min/day)
1. **Comment thoughtfully** on posts from target companies and industry leaders
2. **Share relevant content** with your own perspective
3. **Post updates** about projects, learnings, or career milestones
4. **Congratulate connections** on new roles, promotions, work anniversaries

### Posting Ideas for Students/Early Career
- Project updates: "Just finished my capstone project on [X]. Here's what I learned..."
- Learning journeys: "Spent the weekend learning [skill]. Here's my first attempt..."
- Industry observations: "Interesting trend I noticed in [industry]..."
- Career fair recaps: "Great conversations at [Fair] today. Excited about [Company]'s work in..."
- Gratitude posts: "Thank you to [Professor/Mentor] for helping me understand [concept]..."

### Why This Works
- **Impressions** = More recruiters see your name
- **Profile views** increase from engagement
- **You become memorable** before you even apply
- **Demonstrates communication skills** that employers value

# PHASE 5: THE ICEBERG PROTOCOL (Interview Preparation)

## 5.1 The Master Manuscript

### The Problem
Candidates rely on memory and vague bullet points. They forget details under pressure.

### The Method
Write every detail down in extreme depth BEFORE you edit.

### The Process
For every significant experience, write a "brain dump" document covering:
- Full context and background
- Every action you took (in order)
- Every tool, method, or resource you used
- Every result and outcome
- Every lesson learned
- Every person involved and your interactions with them

### Why It Works
By writing in "extreme detail," you:
- Discover angles you didn't know you had
- Create a database of raw material adaptable to any competency
- Build confidence by knowing your stories cold
- Prepare for unexpected follow-up questions

---

## 5.2 The 5-Star Quality Filter

### The Problem
Candidates tell "average" stories. They use experiences that check the box but don't impress.

### The Rating System

| Rating | Definition | Action |
|--------|------------|--------|
| ★☆☆☆☆ | Horrible. Doesn't answer the question. | DISCARD |
| ★★☆☆☆ | Subpar. Hard to follow, no clear impact. | DISCARD |
| ★★★☆☆ | Sufficient. Checks the box, nothing memorable. | DISCARD |
| ★★★★☆ | Impressive. Clear competency, went above and beyond. | KEEP |
| ★★★★★ | Rock star. Expanded influence, trained others, created multiplier effect. | GOLD |

### The Target
For a 1-hour interview with 5-7 questions:
- **15 total examples** (10 five-star, 5 four-star)
- **Never use 1-3 star examples**
- **Never invent content**

---

## 5.3 The Competency Matrix

### The Problem
Candidates have stories but can't connect them to what interviewers are asking.

### The Framework
Create a grid:
- **X-axis:** Common competencies employers test (Leadership, Problem-Solving, Teamwork, Communication, Technical Skills, Initiative, Adaptability)
- **Y-axis:** Your 15 prepared stories

### The Rule
Every competency must have at least 2 stories mapped to it.

### Example Matrix
| Story | Leadership | Problem-Solving | Teamwork | Communication | Technical |
|-------|------------|-----------------|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Capstone fixture redesign | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | | ✓ |
| Internship automation project | | ✓ | | ✓ | ✓ |
| Student org event planning | ✓ | | ✓ | ✓ | |
| Research assistant work | | ✓ | | ✓ | ✓ |

---

## 5.4 Spaced Repetition Practice

### The Problem
Cramming the night before doesn't work. You'll freeze under pressure.

### The Method
Frequency > Duration

### The Practice Schedule
**Week 1-2 (Example Preparation):**
- Go through all 15 examples morning, noon, and night
- Practice until details are ingrained "like your own last name"

**Week 2+ (Mock Interviews):**
- 1-2 full mock interviews per day
- Use anyone: friends, family, mentors, colleagues
- Even non-experts give valuable feedback on clarity and communication

### Why Deep Memorization Works
Once your examples are memorized to extreme detail, you have an "ocean of content" that can be molded to fit ANY question. With 15 deeply-detailed examples, there's no competency question you can't address from some angle.

# PHASE 6: THE HIGH-CONTRAST INTERVIEW (Performance)

## 6.1 The Power Pause

### The Problem
Nervous candidates talk to fill silence. They answer the literal question rather than the subtext.

### The Method
Take 1-2 minutes to "scan" for the hidden competency before speaking.

### The Script
> "That's a great question. Let me take a moment to think of the most relevant example."

### The Wrap-Around Technique
If you need more time or want to come back to something:
> "I want to make sure I give you a thorough answer. Would it be okay if I came back to this one at the end?"

### Why It Works
- Silence is a power move, not confusion
- Shows confidence and thoughtfulness
- Frames the interview as a collaboration, not an interrogation
- Gives you time to pick the best example

---

## 6.2 Paint the Pain (The 'S' in STAR)

### The Problem
Candidates gloss over the Situation: "We had a bug."

### The Method
Emphasize the pain to make the cure look valuable.

### The Principle: High-Contrast Storytelling
The bigger the monster, the bigger the hero.

| Weak Situation | Strong Situation |
|----------------|------------------|
| "The code was slow" | "The code latency was threatening our SLA with a major client, risking a $50K contract penalty" |
| "We had a tight deadline" | "We had 3 weeks to deliver a 3-month project after scope tripled mid-sprint" |
| "There was a design flaw" | "A critical design flaw was causing 15% field failure rate, with $200K in warranty claims" |

---

## 6.3 The Numbered Roadmap (The 'A' in STAR)

### The Problem
Rambling, jumping back and forth in time. Interviewers lose track.

### The Method
Open your Action section with a number.

### The Script
> "I took 5 key actions to address this. First, I... Second, I... Third..."

### Why It Works
- Forces the interviewer to listen for structure
- Keeps YOU from getting lost
- Shows organized thinking
- Makes your answer memorable

---

## 6.4 The 3-Tier Impact Ladder (The 'R' in STAR)

### The Problem
Candidates stop at "I solved the problem." This is the minimum.

### The Framework
Push your Result up the ladder:

| Level | The Result | What It Signals |
|-------|------------|-----------------|
| **Level 1** | "I solved the problem" | "I am a competent worker" |
| **Level 2** | "I automated it so it won't happen again" | "I think about efficiency and process" |
| **Level 3** | "I trained the team and evangelized the solution" | "I am a future leader and force multiplier" |

### Example Progression
- **Level 1:** "I fixed the bug that was causing the latency issue."
- **Level 2:** "I fixed the bug and implemented monitoring to catch similar issues before they impact production."
- **Level 3:** "I fixed the bug, implemented monitoring, and created a troubleshooting runbook that the team now uses. I also presented the root cause at our engineering all-hands to prevent similar issues across other teams."

### The Goal
Always try to get to Level 2 or 3. That's what separates "hire" from "strong hire."

# PHASE 7: THE SECRET WEAPONS

## 7.1 The Visual Aid Binder

### The Origin Story
For my second-round interview at John Deere headquarters, I prepared a visual aid binder. When I pulled it out in the waiting room, I heard murmurs: "Dang, that's a really good idea. I wish I would have thought of that."

### What to Include
For each of your 15 prepared examples, gather:
- Charts and diagrams
- Visual descriptions of projects
- Design examples (CAD screenshots, schematics)
- Results visualized (before/after, graphs)

### How to Organize
- Professional binder with tabbed sections
- One tab per major example/project
- Clear labels matching your prepared stories

### How to Use It
1. Hand the binder to interviewers at the beginning
2. When answering a question, reference it: "This reminds me of Project X—if you turn to that tab..."
3. As you describe the project, they can see what you're talking about

### Why It Works
- Engages their eyes, not just their ears
- Proves you're an "engineer who documents"
- Differentiates you from every other candidate
- Bridges gaps between your experience and the role (like my civil→mechanical transition)

---

## 7.2 The Cheat Sheet

### The Problem
You can't memorize everything perfectly. Nervousness causes blanks.

### The Tactic
Bring a transparent, bulleted list of your prepared examples.

### How to Use It
1. Ask permission: "I've prepared some notes to make sure I give you thorough answers. Would it be okay if I reference them?"
2. This shows humility and preparation
3. Glance at it to jog your memory, but don't read from it

### Why It Works
- Reduces anxiety (you have a safety net)
- Shows preparation and organization
- Interviewers appreciate candidates who take it seriously

---

## 7.3 The Human Element

### The Principle
People don't want to hire someone they don't want to work with.

### The Tactic
You can't just answer questions and check boxes. Remember:
- These people will work with you day after day
- Show that working with you will be enjoyable
- Be professional, but also be personable
- Ask about THEIR backgrounds—adjust your terminology and examples accordingly

### Questions to Ask Interviewers
- "What brought you to [Company]?"
- "What's your favorite part about working here?"
- "What does success look like for someone in this role?"
- "What's a project you're excited about right now?"

# PHASE 8: THE FINISH LINE

## 8.1 Entry-Level Negotiation Basics

### The Mindset Problem
Most new engineers think:
- "I can't negotiate at entry level"
- "They'll rescind the offer if I ask"
- "I'll seem greedy"

### The Truth
- Attempting to negotiate shows maturity and self-advocacy
- Offers are rarely rescinded for professional negotiation attempts
- Even if they say no, you've set a tone that you value yourself

### What to Negotiate
At entry level, you may not move base salary much. But you CAN ask about:
- Signing bonus
- Relocation assistance
- Start date flexibility
- PTO or vacation days
- Professional development budget
- Remote work flexibility

### How to Ask
> "I'm very excited about this offer. Before I accept, I wanted to ask—is there any flexibility on [signing bonus / start date / etc.]?"

### If They Say No
> "I understand. I appreciate you considering it. I'm still very excited about the opportunity and look forward to proving my value. Would it be possible to revisit compensation after 6 months or 1 year based on performance?"

### Why This Works
- Plants the seed that you expect to be a high performer
- Shows you're thinking long-term
- Leaves the door open for future conversations
- Even a "no" doesn't hurt you

---

## 8.2 Post-Interview Follow-Up

### The 24-Hour Rule
Send a thank-you email within 24 hours of your interview.

### What to Include
1. Thank them for their time
2. Reference something specific you discussed
3. Reiterate your interest and fit
4. Express enthusiasm for next steps

### Example Email
> Subject: Thank You - [Role] Interview
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [Role] position. I really enjoyed learning about [specific project or topic you discussed].
>
> Our conversation reinforced my excitement about joining [Company]. My experience with [relevant skill/project] aligns well with the challenges you mentioned, and I'm eager to contribute to the team.
>
> Please let me know if you need any additional information from me. I look forward to hearing about next steps.
>
> Best regards,
> [Your Name]
