★ Verdict: "You Did What Was Asked"

Your resume right now focuses on what you did but doesn't yet show what happened because of it. You led an 8-person team across two prototype deliveries — that's genuinely impressive for a student — but the resume hasn't given that impact the room it deserves, because process language like "facilitated," "coordinated," and "applied frameworks" takes center stage. The Transferable Skills section, which takes up about 40% of the page, restates your experience in a more general way instead of adding new evidence. The raw material here is strong: 88% WAM, Dean's List twice, EWB Challenge Top 3, a real consulting internship. This is a presentation problem, not a substance problem — and that's the easier kind to fix.

Key Issues Identified

1

The "Transferable Skills" section is taking up space that could be working much harder for you

This section takes up roughly 40% of your resume's real estate, and the problem is that each line makes a claim without backing it up with evidence. For example, "Demonstrated ability to respond effectively to changing client requirements" — a hiring manager reading this will think "says who?" You want to show skills through your results, not state them as standalone claims. The good news is that much of what's described here is already covered in your internship bullets — it just needs to be told through specific outcomes instead of restated in a separate section. Removing this section and reallocating the space to results-driven content is the single biggest improvement you can make.

2

Your internship bullets describe the process well — now they need the results

Your four internship bullets — "Led... delivering two prototypes... on schedule," "Facilitated weekly client meetings," "Applied design thinking frameworks," "Coordinated interdependent timelines" — all do a good job explaining what you did. The missing piece is what happened because of it. You delivered two prototypes — did the client adopt them? Did they solve the problem? Was the client spending $[X] on the old process? Did your solution reduce something, improve something, save something? Adding that Why to each bullet is what turns "I did this" into "I made this happen."

3

Career Objective section isn't helping you stand out

"Motivated Biomedical Engineering student with a strong sense of curiosity about the medical industry" — the challenge here is that every applicant to a biomedical role could say the same thing. Career objectives were standard practice years ago, but today this space would serve you better as a 2-line professional summary that states your specific target role, your strongest accomplishment, and one differentiator (like your EWB Top 3 placement or your consulting experience). Alternatively, you could remove it entirely and let your experience speak for itself.

4

Your technical skills would be stronger shown in action

You have solid tools — SolidWorks, KNIME, C, Java, Python — but right now they're listed without appearing in any of your experience bullets. A hiring manager wants to see how you used them. Compare: "SolidWorks" on a list vs. "Modeled a [X]-component biomedical prototype in SolidWorks, iterating through [X] design revisions based on client feedback." The second version proves you actually know the tool. Try weaving your tools into your bullets so they pull double duty — showing the skill and the result at the same time. For Python, since you note it's "actively developing," it would be stronger to either reference a project you've started building with it, or hold off on listing it until you can.

5

There's an opportunity to add impact metrics throughout

The numbers you do have — "8-member team," "12-week timeline," "four project outcomes" — are good for showing scale. The next step is adding numbers that show impact: a percentage improvement, a time reduction, an efficiency gain, an error rate that dropped. Your internship delivered two prototypes to a real client — what changed for them? Did a process get faster? Did a manual step get automated? Did accuracy improve? Even conservative estimates ("reduced manual processing time by approximately [X]%") make a big difference. You don't need exact figures — thoughtful approximations work.

6

EWB Challenge Top 3 Finalist deserves more spotlight

Being selected as a Top Three Finalist in the Engineers Without Borders Challenge and presenting at the UTS FEIT Capstone Showcase is one of the strongest things on your resume. Right now it's a one-line bullet in "Awards" — and it deserves more room to breathe. What was the project? What problem did you solve for the Dawul Wuru Aboriginal Corporation? What was your engineering contribution? Expanding this into a full project entry with Impact Bullet Builder bullets would give you a section that proves you can engineer solutions to real problems for real communities.

7

High school entry can be removed to free up space

You're in your 4th year of a Bachelor of Engineering at UTS with an 88% WAM and two Dean's List recognitions — your university credentials speak for themselves. At this stage, the secondary school certificate from 2022 isn't adding to your story for a hiring manager. Removing it frees up space you can use for a project entry or additional experience details.

8

Activities section would be stronger with specific contributions

IEEE, Engineers Australia, Australian Bioinformatics and Computational Biology Society, Lucy Mentoring Program — you've clearly been engaged beyond the classroom, which is great. The next step is showing what you did within these organizations. Did you attend any conferences? Present anything? Organize a workshop? Mentor anyone? If you contributed something specific in any of these, add a bullet for it. For the ones where it's purely a membership, consider whether the space might serve you better elsewhere.
