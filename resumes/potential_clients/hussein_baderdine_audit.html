You've done something most entry-level engineers haven't — you tailored this resume to a specific role, used strong action verbs throughout, and kept personal ownership in every bullet. That gives you a real head start. But the resume is scoring low on the two factors that matter most to hiring managers: measurable outcomes and business impact. 02

Key Issues Identified

1

The "Why" is almost completely missing — 9 of 11 bullets have no business outcome

This is the single biggest driver of your 56/100 score. The Impact Bullet Builder formula requires every bullet to explain why the result mattered — cost saved, time reduced, revenue generated, users impacted, risk mitigated. Most of your bullets stop right before that step. "Delivered 6+ user stories per sprint" tells me what you accomplished. But what did those user stories enable? What shipped? What was the business outcome for ASSA ABLOY? Without that final clause, hiring managers can't distinguish your output from any other intern's. This one fix across your 8 affected bullets would raise your score to approximately 76/100.

2

Only 3 of 11 bullets have a real number — 8 bullets are unquantified

Your three quantified bullets are strong: "3 anchor clients within the first 30 days," "6+ user stories per sprint at a consistent 5-point average," and "sub-200ms sync latency." The other 8 have no number at all. "Deployed front-end improvements... that reduced page load latency for factory operators" — by how much? 10ms? 500ms? 60%? The number doesn't have to be dramatic to work; it just needs to exist. A conservative, honest estimate is better than nothing, because "nothing" reads as either unmeasured or unmemorable. Every bullet that can take a number should have one.

3

The Odoo positioning commentary is consuming the space where results should live

Seven of your bullets end with a phrase that explains why the experience is relevant to Odoo — "directly mirroring Odoo's AE model," "gaining firsthand ERP and enterprise software context to draw from when advising Odoo prospects," "replicating the project management and demo-delivery rhythm Odoo AEs execute with every prospect." The intent is smart. The execution works against you. Hiring managers already know why they're reading your resume. These phrases burn 15–20 words on advocacy the reader doesn't need — words that could instead be a specific metric or a business outcome. Cut the Odoo commentary. Put a number there instead.

4

Six bullets describe activities, not accomplishments

Take "Bridged technical and business conversations with decision-makers ranging from architects to procurement managers, developing the multi-stakeholder fluency central to enterprise SaaS sales." That describes something you did repeatedly — not a result of doing it. What deal closed because of that conversation? What proposal got approved? What relationship turned into revenue? The IBB accomplishment test is simple: did something change because of this bullet? If the answer is "not explicitly," it's still a duty description. Apply the test to every bullet that doesn't contain a result statement.

5

The quality gap between your best and worst bullets is too wide

Bullet 3 from Upscale Interiors — "Built a structured outreach pipeline... generating repeat business from 3 anchor clients within the first 30 days" — is a genuine ★★★ bullet. It has a method, a metric, a timeframe, and a business result. Then the very next bullet from the same role is a ★ duty bullet with zero metrics. A hiring manager reading consecutively loses confidence that the strong bullet represents your standard. The goal isn't to rewrite your worst bullets up to ★★★ on the first try — it's to bring every bullet to at least ★★ so there's no cliff drop in quality from one line to the next.

Top Action Items

1

Strip every "...which translates to Odoo" phrase and replace it with a number

Go through every bullet and find the phrases that end with role-relevance commentary — "directly mirroring Odoo's AE model," "to draw from when advising Odoo prospects," "Odoo AEs execute with every prospect." Delete them. In that freed-up space, put the result you were avoiding: how many contracts, what revenue, what efficiency gain, what user count. This single pass would affect 7 of your 11 bullets and would be the highest-ROI 30 minutes you spend on this resume.

2

Add at least one number to every bullet that doesn't have one

8 of your 11 bullets have no specific number. Go through each one and ask yourself: how many? how much? how fast? how often? how long did it take? You don't need exact data — a defensible estimate with honest rounding works. "Approximately $X in revenue," "roughly X% faster" is far stronger than no number at all. Write down your best guess, and adjust if you're asked about it in an interview. Being able to explain your estimate is actually impressive.

3

Expand CyFinance from 1 bullet to 2–3 bullets with outcomes

You led a 4-person team through a full Agile process to build a mobile finance app with a SpringBoot backend, MySQL database, and GitLab CI/CD — that's multiple distinct accomplishments compressed into one underpowered bullet. Separate the leadership story from the technical story from the delivery outcome. Three bullets at ★★ are stronger than one bullet at ★. At minimum, add a second bullet that describes what the app actually does, how many users it supports, and what the CI/CD pipeline enabled.

4

Apply the "So What?" test to every bullet before finalizing

After writing each bullet, read it aloud and ask "So what?" If you can hear a logical follow-up answer that belongs in the bullet but isn't there, add it. "Deployed CI/CD improvements that reduced page load latency for factory operators" → so what? → operators could complete their input tasks faster, reducing downtime per shift. That answer belongs in the bullet. This test takes 10 seconds per bullet and catches the "almost there" cases that are holding your score down.

5

Gut the professional summary or cut it entirely

Your summary currently reads as a cover letter paragraph — "the exact profile Odoo Account Executives are built on." That's persuasive language for a cover letter, but on a resume it feels like telling the hiring manager what to think instead of showing them evidence. Either replace the summary with 2–3 tight bullet points of your top quantified achievements (what a recruiter would skim for in 6 seconds), or remove it and let your experience section lead. The summary as written is not adding credibility — the bullets you haven't yet quantified would do more.
