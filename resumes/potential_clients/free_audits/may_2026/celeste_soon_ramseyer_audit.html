Key Issues Identified

1

7 of 8 bullets have no specific number — one exceptional bullet is doing all the quantification work

Your Python performance model bullet is a masterclass in quantified engineering results: 13.8 kN average thrust, 60,425 N·s total impulse, 0.71 s ignition, 10,450 m apogee, +14.3% beyond specification. That bullet alone is why your Metrics score isn't a 1. But every other bullet on the resume has zero numbers, including your entire work experience section, your UQ research, your water channel research, and both personal projects. For academic candidates, the IBB framework accepts research-appropriate metrics: how many wind tunnel test cases did your pipeline automate? What was the uncertainty range before vs. after your methodology? How many consoles have you repaired? What Reynolds number range did you confirm? You have the data — it just isn't on the resume.

2

Scale and scope are invisible on 6 of 8 bullets — the work is impressive but a hiring manager can't gauge it

Context framing asks: how big was this? What was at stake? Your Mk-41 VLS-compatible framing in the rocket motor bullet plants a clear flag — that's a real Navy weapon system standard. But everywhere else, the scale is unclear. At the NDA technology company, how many assembly procedures did you translate? How many factory floor workers depended on that documentation? For the airfoil research: how many external submitters were you providing results to? For the water channel: what was the Reynolds number range you validated? Scope statements don't require exact proprietary numbers — even approximate figures or relative context ("supporting [X] active external aerodynamics clients," "Re range of [X] to [Y]") make the work land with a reader who doesn't know your lab.

3

"Collaborated with a PhD researcher" undersells your actual technical contribution

The water channel bullet describes real, hands-on analytical work: you set up flow visualization experiments, calculated Reynolds numbers in MATLAB, and validated the experimental setup. That's not assisting — that's contributing core technical labor to a research project. But "Collaborated with a PhD researcher" frames you as a supporting player on someone else's study. The rewrite is simple: lead with the technical action you personally performed. "Calculated Reynolds numbers and confirmed laminar flow agreement in MATLAB" is what you did — the PhD researcher context matters less than the fact that you ran the analysis and your results validated the setup. Changing the opener alone moves this bullet from ★★ to ★★★ territory once you add a number.

4

Personal project bullets are present tense and describe activities rather than outcomes

"Perform SMD component replacement and trace-level PCB rework on handheld gaming consoles" — present tense framing ("Perform," "Execute") tells a hiring manager this is something you do rather than something you've accomplished. There's no outcome: how many consoles have you repaired? What was the success rate? What's the most complex repair you've done? Similarly, the modular synthesizer bullet names the tools (SolidWorks/Fusion360, FDM, SLA) but doesn't say whether you finished it, how many modules it has, or what it does. Personal projects exist on an engineering resume to show initiative and technical range — they land much harder when they read like completed technical work, not an ongoing hobby description.

Top Action Items

1

Add numbers to your top 4 research/project bullets — your rocket motor bullet proves you know how to do this

The Python performance model bullet has 5 specific engineering numbers and beats specification by 14.3% — it is the strongest single bullet on this resume. Apply the same logic to your UQ research, water channel work, and FSAE capsule. For UQ: how many test cases or external submitters? What was the uncertainty improvement? For water channel: what Reynolds number range, what was the measured-vs-calculated agreement within? These numbers exist from your lab notebooks or reports. Putting them on your resume closes the only major gap between a 66 and an 85+.

2

Rewrite the water channel bullet — own the MATLAB analysis, don't frame yourself as the PhD's assistant

The technical work you did in that research (reynolds number calculation, experimental validation, data agreement analysis) is exactly what engineering employers want to see. Starting with "Collaborated with a PhD researcher" buries your contribution under someone else's credential. Start with what you personally ran: "Conducted laminar flow visualization experiments and calculated Reynolds numbers in MATLAB..." — then add the PhD research program as context at the end, not at the start. This single change moves the opener from a support framing to a technical ownership framing.

3

Convert your personal project bullets from present-tense activity to past-tense accomplishment

"Perform" and "Execute" frame your personal projects as ongoing habits rather than completed technical work. Every resume bullet should be past tense and describe something you did, not something you do. Beyond the tense fix: both personal project bullets need an outcome. For the consoles: how many have you repaired? What's your completion rate? For the synthesizer: is it working? How many modules? What voltage rails? Treating your personal projects with the same rigor as your academic work signals to technical interviewers that you approach your own engineering with professional discipline.

4

Add scale to your NDA work experience bullet — even rough estimates are credible and expected

The NDA constraint means you can't name the company, but it doesn't prevent you from describing scale. "Translated technical work instructions... for the China-side manufacturing team" — how many staff on that team? How many procedure documents did you translate or digitize? Even approximate figures ("20+ factory floor staff," "standardized [X] process documents") anchor the scope of your contribution. The translation and documentation work you did has real business stakes (misunderstood QC instructions in a manufacturing environment can cause defects or recalls) — the bullet should say so, even obliquely.
