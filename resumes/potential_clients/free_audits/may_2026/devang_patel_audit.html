02

Key Issues Identified

1

All three Deepak Nitrite bullets describe what you observed — not what you did or what changed

The three bullets from your Deepak Nitrite internship read as a process operator log: "Operated and monitored batch chemical processing units," "Conducted daily plant walkthroughs... observed and documented," "Performed data logging and production record documentation." Every verb describes activity or observation, and none states what the work produced. This is a 4-month role at a 2,760 MT/year pharmaceutical intermediates facility — a real, high-stakes chemical environment. The takeaway a hiring manager gets from these bullets is: you went to the plant, you watched things happen, and you wrote them down. That's a record of attendance, not a record of engineering contribution. Even for an internship, there's almost always something you identified, improved, flagged, or enabled. What deviations did you catch and escalate? Did your documentation support any quality release decisions? Was there anything in the production records that caused an action to be taken? That's what belongs in the bullet.

2

The safety work at Epigral (PTW, PSSR/HIRA) has scale but no outcome — 30+ permits/day is operationally impressive, but what was the result?

Issuing 30+ permits to work per day at India's first bio-based ECH facility is a high-volume, high-consequence safety workload. PSSR and HIRA involvement in 10+ project modifications is substantive safety engineering — these aren't checkbox exercises, they're structured hazard identification processes. But both bullets report the activity count and stop there. The "why" is entirely missing: what was the safety record during your time in that role? Did the facility maintain LTI-free operations? Were there modifications that you identified a hazard in and got changed before startup? The PTW bullet says you supervised hot works "to ensure safe work practices" — but every permit issuer says that. What actually happened? Even a general statement like "maintaining zero hot-work incidents over [X] months" or "supporting incident-free restart across all 10 modifications" turns a process description into a result statement.

3

"Actively participated in PSSR and HIRA reviews" sells short work that takes real engineering judgment

PSSR (Pre-Startup Safety Review) and HIRA (Hazard Identification and Risk Assessment) are structured, rigorous processes — you can't just show up and sign off. Being involved in 10+ of these for project modifications means you were sitting in rooms making technical safety calls about what was and wasn't acceptable before restarting plant operations. "Actively participated" frames that as attendance. What was your actual role? Did you lead any of the walkthroughs? Were you the one verifying corrective actions were closed before restart? Did you flag anything that wasn't ready? The rewrite is straightforward: replace "participated" with the specific thing you did, and add one outcome. "Coordinated pre-restart compliance verification" and "ensured all 10 modifications restarted without safety non-conformances" would be clean and specific.

4

The ECH production bullet names specs but doesn't say what maintaining those specs required or prevented

"Operated high-pressure furnace, vacuum distillation, and azeotropic steam stripping to produce pharmaceutical-grade ECH (≥99.9% purity, <500 ppm water, <0.01% residual DCP)" is technically strong — the spec numbers are real, the equipment is named. But it reads as a job description for any process operator at that facility. What made this work hard? What happened when parameters drifted? Were there any batch rejections or near-misses that you caught before they became out-of-spec product? Even framing this around throughput ("maintaining pharmaceutical-grade purity across [X] production batches over [Y] months") or consistency ("achieving 100% on-spec batch release for downstream API synthesis") turns a list of specifications into a demonstrated result. The high-pressure and azeotropic separation work already signals technical complexity — the bullet just needs to say what that complexity delivered.
