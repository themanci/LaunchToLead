02

Key Issues Identified

1

Splitting duties and achievements into two separate sections buries your wins and cuts your impact in half

For each role, your CV lists responsibilities first — "Provided technical support," "Managed subscription renewals," "Developed and maintained training materials" — and then a separate "Achievements at Vantage" section below. In practice, a recruiter skims the responsibilities, forms an impression of a support technician, and either stops reading or arrives at the Achievements section having already placed you in a box. Neither outcome serves you. The best-performing resumes don't separate duties from results — they lead with results. The 73%/32%/12% subscription growth figures are among the most compelling numbers on your CV — they should be the first bullet under Vantage, not a footnote after eight lines of job description.

2

Your best achievement bullets are missing the "How" — a 73% subscription growth number with no explanation of method leaves a hiring manager guessing

"Grew software subscriptions numbers by 73% in 2022, 32% in 2023 and 12% in 2024" is a strong result — sustained multi-year growth at a SaaS product is exactly the kind of data point that gets CVs read twice. But the bullet stops at the number. A hiring manager who sees 73% growth immediately wants to know: what did you actually do? Was it the new onboarding workflow you built? The marketing events you led? The self-service training resource you spearheaded? You know the answer — the bullet just doesn't say it. The same issue applies to the enrolment growth bullets in your earlier roles. Numbers without method read as reporting, not ownership. Hiring managers want to see that you understand the mechanism behind your results, not just that the metric moved.

3

"Dozens," "hundreds," and "significantly" are failing as metrics — they read as estimates, not evidence

"Led marketing events, acquiring dozens of new customers through targeted outreach" — "dozens" is a vague quantifier that fails the IBB metric test. The same applies to "Updated hundreds of product prices in NetSuite ERP efficiently" and "Developed an Excel-based pricing system, reducing manual errors significantly." All three of these bullets clearly have a real number behind them — you know how many customers came from those events, how many SKUs you updated in NetSuite, and what the error reduction actually looked like. Precise numbers convert a claim into evidence. "Dozens" doesn't tell a hiring manager whether you acquired 12 new customers or 90. That ambiguity costs you credibility on bullets that should be among your strongest.

4

The "Key Competencies" section is using prime CV real estate for claims that can't be verified

Your CV opens with twelve bullet points of soft-skill descriptions: "Proficient in gathering and translating user requirements," "Expert in stakeholder communication," "Proven track record in delivering marketing strategies." Every one of these is something any candidate could copy-paste onto their CV without ever having done it. What makes them real isn't saying them — it's showing them in your experience bullets with specific examples. The space used by this section delays the hiring manager from seeing any evidence of what you've achieved. The competencies aren't wrong — they just belong as proof points inside your work bullets, not as assertions at the top of the page. Replace this section with a focused 3-sentence professional summary that leads with your strongest metric.

5

Almost nothing connects a result to why it mattered for the business — your wins stop before the finish line

The Impact Bullet Builder has four components: Accomplishment, Metric, How, and Why. The "Why" is the business outcome — not just what happened, but what the business gained because of it. Your strongest bullet comes closest: "Redesigned company website, increasing visitors and inquiries by 30% and orders by 50%" — the 50% orders increase is the business outcome. That's the only bullet with a Why. Everything else stops before it. "Grew software subscriptions numbers by 73%" — what did that mean for the company? Was that $[X] in new recurring revenue? Did it hit a board target? Did it enable Vantage to expand their service territory? Connecting the metric to a business consequence transforms a number into a business accomplishment a hiring manager can use to evaluate your impact.

Top Action Items

1

Collapse the duties-vs-achievements split into a single bullet list per role, and lead with results

The current two-section structure means a hiring manager reads eight duty descriptions before seeing any of your wins. Restructure each role to 5–7 bullets that are all achievement-oriented: start with your biggest result for that role, then describe how you operated day-to-day in a way that shows ownership, not just activity. The 73% subscription growth bullet should be the first line under Vantage — that's the number that earns the second look. Every other bullet should show what you built, changed, or achieved, not just what your role included.

2

Replace every vague quantifier with the real number — you know them, use them

"Dozens of new customers," "hundreds of product prices," "significantly" — each of these is a metric that almost passes. Look up the actual counts: how many customers came from those marketing events? How many SKUs did you update in NetSuite? What was the pricing error reduction? If you don't have the exact figure, use a range: "25–35 new customers" is more credible than "dozens" and still passes the IBB metric test. Precise numbers are what separate CVs that get called back from ones that get screened out in the first pass.

3

Add a "How" clause to your five biggest achievement bullets

Your best achievement bullets have strong metrics but no method. For the 73% subscription growth: what specifically changed? Was it the onboarding workflow you rebuilt? The marketing events? The self-service training page you spearheaded? For the 100% enrolment increase: what approach or campaign drove that result? Pick the one or two things most responsible for each result and name them in the bullet. This is the difference between "the number went up" and "I know why the number went up and I can do it again" — which is exactly what a hiring manager is evaluating.

4

Remove the Key Competencies section and replace it with a 3-sentence professional summary

The twelve-point competencies list works against you in two ways: it delays the hiring manager from reaching your actual achievements, and it makes claims without evidence (anyone can write "Expert in stakeholder communication"). If any of those competencies are genuinely differentiating — and some are, like your API integration and SOP development work — they belong as proof inside your experience bullets. Replace the section with a focused summary: your specialisation, your strongest result, and what type of role you're targeting. Three sentences, written in first person, with at least one number.

5

Add one business-impact sentence to your top five bullets — the "Why" that completes the story

The Why answers: what did the business gain? Revenue generated, customers retained, time saved, cost reduced, or scale reached. Your 50% orders increase from the website redesign already has this — it's your only ★★★ bullet for this reason. For the subscription growth: was that $[X]K in new recurring revenue? For the Excel pricing system: did it save [Y] hours per week? For the Trimble self-learning resource: how many customers used it, and did it reduce inbound support calls? One sentence per bullet connects your work to business value and separates your CV from every candidate who only reported what they did.
