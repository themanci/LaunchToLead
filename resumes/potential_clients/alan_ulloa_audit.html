02

Key Issues Identified

1

No stakes or context framing — 13 of 16 bullets have no "before" state, no scope, no scale

This is the single biggest driver keeping your score at 60. The IBB framework requires every bullet to include a context layer — what was the scope of the system, how complex was the environment, what was at risk if you didn't solve this, or what did "before" look like. Right now, bullets like "Maintained a plan of record for program milestones, tracked costs and hardware receipt status, and coordinated space and resource allocation decisions across manufacturing stakeholders to support integration schedules" describe the activity in isolation. A hiring manager can't gauge the scale of what you managed. Was this 3 milestones or 30? Were you tracking $50K or $5M in hardware costs? Adding scope statements or before/after comparisons — even short ones — to your top 6 bullets alone would raise your score from 60 to approximately 78.

2

Only 5 of 16 bullets have a specific number — 11 are unquantified

Your Medtronic role shows exactly what happens when you include numbers: "65+ controlled experiments" and "78.4% increase in battery production throughput" are immediately credible and memorable. Your Bloom Energy role has "20+ engineer cross-functional team" and "training 7 engineers" — those land, too. But your Applied Materials bullets — your most recent and supposedly strongest signal — have zero numbers anywhere. How many program milestones were you tracking? How many engineering design reviews did you coordinate per quarter? How many stakeholders across how many campuses? Even rough estimates framed honestly work. "Tracked [X] program milestones across [Y] cross-functional teams" is infinitely stronger than the same sentence without numbers.

3

Your Applied Materials bullets describe the job, not what you achieved in it

Applied Materials is your most recent, most senior role — it will get the most scrutiny. Right now, all four bullets read like an expanded job description. "Maintained program status and drove accountability across engineering, operations, and quality teams — tracking deliverables, managing schedule dependencies, and surfacing technical and programmatic blockers" tells a hiring manager what the role involved, not what you specifically influenced or moved. Compare that to your Medtronic bullet, which has a real before→after swing (78.4% throughput increase). For the TPM role, the question is: did your programs land on time? Did your escalation process shorten the average blocker resolution cycle? Did your leadership reports change a decision? One outcome-framed statement per role is the target.

4

No PM tools named in your Applied Materials role — the method layer is invisible

The IBB "How" component asks: what specific tool, technology, method, or process did you use? Your projects section nails this — SolidWorks, Fusion 360, Minitab, 5 Whys, Fishbone are all named. But your Applied Materials and Bloom Energy work experience bullets have almost no named tools. You listed Smartsheet in your skills section, but it never shows up in an accomplishment. Did you track program milestones in Smartsheet? Build dashboards in Excel? Manage dependencies in a specific scheduling tool? A single sentence like "...tracking [X] open action items in Smartsheet across [Y] stakeholder teams" tells a hiring manager both what you did and how you did it. That methodological specificity is what separates a strong TPM resume from a generic PM resume.

5

One "Supported" opener at Honeywell is obscuring real work

"Supported operations planning and process validation by designing automation tools, improving data transfer efficiency, and visibility across production systems" — the word "Supported" frames you as a helper on someone else's work instead of the person who built something. You designed the automation tools. You improved data transfer. That's the active work. The fix is straightforward: lead with the action you owned, not the role you played in someone else's initiative. "Designed and deployed [X] automation tools that improved data transfer efficiency by [Y]% across [Z] production systems" flips this from an assist credit to an ownership statement.

Top Action Items

1

Add scale and stakes framing to every Applied Materials bullet

Your most recent and most senior role has zero context framing. Before your next job application, spend 20 minutes answering these questions for that role: How many milestones were you tracking? How many engineers attended your design reviews? How many campuses were involved? What was the cost range of hardware you were tracking? These numbers don't need to be precise — reasonable estimates written confidently are enough. Add one to two scale statements per bullet and your Applied Materials section transforms from a job description to a leadership narrative.

2

Embed Smartsheet (and any other tools you used) directly in your accomplishment bullets

Smartsheet is listed in your skills section, but it never appears in a bullet where you actually used it. The skills section is the least-read part of a resume. The most-read part is work experience. Move your tool names into the context where the work happened: "...tracked milestones in Smartsheet," "...built production schedules using Smartsheet timeline views," etc. This demonstrates tool fluency through application — which is far more credible than a name on a list.

3

Quantify the Bloom Energy and Honeywell bullets you haven't yet touched

You already have quantified bullets at Bloom Energy (20+ engineers) and Medtronic (65+ experiments, 78.4% throughput). Apply the same approach to the bullets that still have nothing: the PCN workflow initiative has no time or effort metric, the Honeywell automation tools have no efficiency number, and the inspections bullet has no volume or impact rate. All three have real numbers behind them — you just need to surface what you actually measured during those internships.

4

Reframe at least one Applied Materials bullet as a specific program outcome

The strongest version of your TPM bullet isn't about what you tracked — it's about what shipped on time because you tracked it. TPMs are judged on program outcomes: did milestones land? Did the product reach manufacturing readiness? Did your escalations prevent schedule slips? If you can identify one program outcome from your time at Applied Materials — even a partial win like "contributed to on-time tape-out for Program X" — lead with that. It reframes the whole role from administrative to outcomes-driven.

5

Consider whether "Program Engineer" or "Technical Program Manager" is your actual target title

Your resume spans two different tracks: hands-on engineering (Medtronic laser bonding, Honeywell 3D printing, SolidWorks project work) and program management (Applied Materials TPM, Bloom Energy PM intern). These are different roles, and a resume that reads as "both" can appear unfocused to a hiring manager. If your target is TPM roles, lead with that identity and push the hands-on technical work below. If your target is "Program Engineer" — a role that combines both — make that explicit in a brief summary sentence at the top so the reader immediately understands the combination as intentional.
