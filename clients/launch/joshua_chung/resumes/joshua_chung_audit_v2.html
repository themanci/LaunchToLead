51/100 puts this resume in the middle tier — solidly above ★-level work, but with a clear ceiling. The tooling and methodology section is perfect: SolidWorks, FEA, GD&T, Roboflow, CNC machining all named throughout. What's holding the score back is a near-total absence of "Why" and zero problem context across all 13 bullets. This resume shows what you built. It doesn't yet show what changed because you built it — and that's the entire gap between ★★ and ★★★.
2 Key Issues Identified
1
"Why" is almost entirely missing — 11 of 13 bullets stop before the business outcome
The IBB formula requires every bullet to close the loop: why did this result matter? Only 2 bullets on this resume do that — the bridge efficiency bullet (structural efficiency ratio of 276) and the Roboflow bullet (cutting preparation time by 20%). Every other bullet ends at the deliverable or the specification, not the consequence. "Resulting in a lightweight design ergonomically suitable for astronaut use" is a design requirement — it's not the same as a stated outcome. What did the lightweight design enable? Faster EVA deployment? Fit within a mass budget that cleared a NASA review gate? That one sentence is the difference between a spec-compliant resume and a standout one.
"Designed a lunar payload subsystem device...resulting in a lightweight and ergonomically suitable for astronaut use" — ends at a requirement, not an outcome.
2
Zero problem context across all 13 bullets
Not a single bullet on this resume frames what you walked into before you fixed it. Problem context is what makes a result feel earned rather than assumed — and it's the lowest-scoring criterion at 1/10. The format is simple: one sentence before the accomplishment that establishes stakes, constraints, or the before-state. "With no existing cam mechanism validated for EVA glove use..." or "On a bridge design that had failed at 1,800g in the prior iteration..." reframes everything that follows. Without context, a hiring manager reads your results and thinks "that's what the project asked for." With context, they read it and think "that's what you solved."
0 of 13 bullets include stakes, before/after framing, or scale context of any kind.
3
Most bullets describe tasks completed, not accomplishments achieved
8 of 13 bullets describe what you did — designed, developed, implemented, performed, created — without stating what changed because you did it. The IBB test is simple: if someone else with the same job title could write this exact bullet, it's a duty description, not an accomplishment. "Performed design verification and testing using FEA and measurement tools validating the device against strict specifications" tells a hiring manager you ran tests. It doesn't tell them what the tests confirmed, what failure modes were eliminated, or what milestone the validation cleared. The fix is almost always one clause: add the outcome of the action, not just the action itself.
"Created documentation and decision matrices, detailing the trade-offs, performance metrics, and clean sheet design process to support subsystem integration" — describes the deliverable with no outcome stated.
4
Metrics missing from 8 of 13 bullets
The two strongest bullets on the resume — the bridge efficiency (5,240g / ratio of 276) and the Roboflow processing (100+ frames, 20% time reduction) — work because they have numbers. Eight other bullets have no specific quantity at all. For capstone and project work, common sources include: load capacity tested, iteration count, mass/weight targets met, tolerances achieved, time to complete a process step, number of components in an assembly, or FEA stress values vs. material limits. "3+ prototype iterations" is a start; "3 prototype iterations that reduced mass by [X]% while meeting NASA's [X]kg mass budget" is the target.
"Developed a cam-based mechanical system and manually operated digging mechanism using Solidworks Simulation ensuring EVA glove compatibility..." — no number, no before/after comparison.

5 Top Action Items
1
Add "Why" to every bullet — what changed because of what you built?
For each bullet, ask one question after the result: "So what?" What did the design enable? What milestone did the test clear? What risk did it eliminate? The answer — even estimated — is your "Why." For NASA work specifically: did the device clear a design review? Meet a mass budget? Pass a fit check? Those outcomes exist; they just need to be stated. This single edit moves more bullets from ★★ to ★★★ than any other change on this resume.
2
Add one sentence of context to each role's strongest bullet
You don't need context on every bullet — start with your best one per role. For the bridge: what was the prior iteration's load capacity before your FEA work? For NASA: what was the design constraint you were working against (mass limit, EVA glove force limit, regolith particle range)? A single framing sentence — "Working within NASA's [X]kg EVA tool mass budget..." — makes everything that follows land harder. Context is what makes results feel earned.
3
Add numbers to the 8 bullets that currently have none
For project and capstone work, look for: load values tested, mass/weight targets, tolerances achieved, FEA stress values vs. material yield limits, number of components in the assembly, time to complete a key process step, or percentage improvement from one iteration to the next. If you genuinely don't have a number, a before/after comparison or a scale statement ("across a 6-member team," "for a 12-week capstone") is better than nothing. Numbers are what interviewers ask about — put them on the page so the conversation starts there.
4
Replace "Collaborated" and "Supported" with specific owned actions
"Collaborated with a team to design, fabricate, and test 3+ bridge iterations" and "Supported validation design efforts" both hide your personal contribution. What specifically did you own? Did you run the FEA? Set up the test rig? Define the load increments? Replacing "Collaborated with a team" with "Owned the FEA analysis across 3 bridge iterations, identifying the truss node geometry changes that..." takes the same fact and makes it yours. "Supported" is on the banned list for a reason — it signals a secondary role even when you weren't playing one.
5
Strengthen the Mechanical Design & Assembly role with outcomes
Both bullets in this role ("Developed engineering drawings... applying GD&T" and "Created structured assembly procedure and BOM") describe deliverables with no stated outcome. What did the GD&T callouts enable — first-pass manufacturability? A specific tolerance stack requirement met? What was the BOM for — how many components, what type of system? One concrete detail per bullet turns these from task descriptions into contribution statements. This role is recent (Feb 2026) and deserves to be as strong as the capstone work.
