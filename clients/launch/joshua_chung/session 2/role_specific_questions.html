4. Deep Dive — Questions 2, 3, and 10

These are the highest-priority questions for the field service role. Safety, communication, and escalation judgment are the core differentiators.

Question 2 — Safety / Compliance Protocols

"Describe a situation where you had to follow strict safety or compliance protocols during a hands-on project. How did you ensure nothing was overlooked?"

They're looking for:

  • Specific awareness of why the protocol exists, not just blind compliance
  • A system or habit for ensuring nothing gets skipped (checklists, peer review, documentation)
  • Evidence that safety isn't something imposed on you — it's something you own

They don't want to see:

  • Vague answers like "I always follow the rules"
  • No concrete example or process described
  • Treating safety as an inconvenience or afterthought
  • Taking credit for safety culture without describing personal action

Best Josh experiences:

  • NASA design verification against strict specs for low-gravity operation — safety-critical testing with clear external standards
  • NASA interface constraints and EVA glove compatibility — designing within non-negotiable safety/usability boundaries

Question 3 — Explaining Technical Decisions to External Audiences

"Give me an example of when you had to explain a technical decision or trade-off to someone outside your immediate team — a professor, sponsor, or external reviewer. How did you make it clear?"

They're looking for:

  • Ability to translate engineering rationale into terms the audience actually cares about
  • Awareness of who the audience is and adjusting accordingly
  • Structured communication — not rambling through every detail

They don't want to see:

  • "I just showed them the data and they got it" — no evidence of tailoring
  • Overcomplicating the explanation or hiding behind jargon
  • No awareness that the audience had different priorities than the engineering team
  • Blaming the audience for not understanding

Best Josh experiences:

  • NASA decision matrices and trade-off documentation — literally built artifacts to communicate engineering rationale to external reviewers
  • NASA capstone as a whole — presenting to NASA stakeholders who care about mission success, not your CAD skills

Question 10 — Solve Independently vs. Escalate

"Tell me about a situation where you had to decide whether to solve a problem on your own or escalate it to someone more experienced. What factors drove your decision?"

They're looking for:

  • A clear framework for the decision — what factors tipped it one way or the other (time pressure, risk level, knowledge gap, impact of getting it wrong)
  • Willingness to ask for help without ego
  • Evidence they attempted to scope the problem before escalating, not just handing it off blindly

They don't want to see:

  • "I always figure it out myself" — in field service, this gets you fired or gets equipment damaged
  • "I always ask my manager" — no independence
  • No reasoning behind the decision, just describing what happened
  • Escalating without bringing any diagnostic information to the senior person

Best Josh experiences:

  • Robotics lab research assistant — junior role working under a PI, natural dynamic of knowing what's in your lane vs. when to loop in the lead researcher
  • NASA capstone with multiple subsystems — had to coordinate across teams, likely hit points where a decision was above his scope
