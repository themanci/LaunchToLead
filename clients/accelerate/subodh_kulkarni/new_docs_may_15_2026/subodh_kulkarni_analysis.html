Key Issues Identified

1

Your two opening bullets at John Deere R&D are duty descriptions — hiring managers read these first

The first two bullets of your most recent role — "Developed DTAC solutions for FEE Frames" and "Analyzed multiple DTAC cases and provided resolution on auger vibration..." — describe activities without stating any result. "Developed solutions" and "provided resolution" are outcomes in spirit but stripped of the data that makes them credible. What percentage of cases reached resolution? How many DTAC cases did you close? What was the before/after failure rate on CutterBar and KD Skid? These are your current-role credentials and they will be read first by every hiring manager. Right now they open with the weakest two bullets on the entire resume — that framing needs to be reversed.

2

Several bullets are dense, multi-sentence paragraphs that lose the reader before they reach the number

Your strongest bullets are also your longest ones. The MY26 bullet runs 5+ lines with four parenthetical asides before reaching the business outcome. The wire harness pre-production bullet is similarly dense. Hiring managers spending 6–10 seconds on a resume will not parse a paragraph — they skim for the number, the role scope, and the result. The solution is not to cut the content (the data is excellent) but to restructure: lead with the number and result, then follow with the method. "Reduced repeat defects by 70% on the MY26 program..." currently appears mid-sentence. That number should be the first four words.

3

One bullet is pure activity with no evaluable content and should be removed

"Worked with multiple growers, leaders in industries, students, professors, and dealers for field trials" — this bullet describes a relationship network with no outcome, no scale, no method, and no impact. It fails 7 of the 8 IBB criteria. In a resume that is otherwise well-quantified, this entry weakens the overall impression by introducing a ★ bullet into a ★★★ portfolio. The context it provides (cross-functional stakeholder engagement) should either be merged into one of the three strong University of Arkansas bullets above it, or cut entirely.

4

One Walchandnagar bullet frames you as a contributor rather than a leader, undermining your seniority positioning

"Worked with third party collaborators on product development and producing prototypes" — at a role titled Deputy Chief Engineer, "worked with collaborators" is a junior framing. The entire bullet reads as supporting context for the bullet above it rather than a standalone accomplishment. If there was a real deliverable here — a design review completed, a prototype validated, a supplier qualification outcome — that should be surfaced. If not, this bullet should be merged into B21 or removed.

5

Your University of Arkansas bullets lack a named method — they're strong on outcomes but thin on how

The three University of Arkansas work experience bullets ($500K grants, $50K/$40K research, 3 educational programs) all have strong numbers and clear outcomes but none names a research approach, analytical method, or tool. For the grant work: what was the research design? For the cotton/ATV research: what measurement or simulation approach did you use? For the educational programs: was content delivered via specific extension curricula or proprietary training materials? A named method lifts these from "I got funded and delivered" to "I ran a specific scientific or pedagogical approach that produced a result." Given the strength of your industry bullets, this is a moderate gap — but it's correctable with a phrase or two per bullet.

6

The Elpro bullet is too thin to support a 30-year portfolio — one sentence with 3% and no why or method

"Increased manufacturing efficiency by 3% as process engineer for medical X-ray machine and press shop" — at this stage of career, a 1-bullet entry with a 3% outcome and no named method or business consequence is a weak close to the work history section. This role was the start of your career, so depth may not be available — but if there was a specific process change you made, or a production constraint you solved, a single sentence of additional context would complete the bullet. Alternatively, if this role doesn't strengthen the overall narrative, consider moving it to a "Earlier Career" single-line footnote.
