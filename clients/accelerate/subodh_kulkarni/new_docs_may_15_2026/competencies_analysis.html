Key Pattern Across All Stories

The Actions sections are detailed and strong — there is real depth and personal ownership throughout. The consistent weakness is the Results section. Across the file, results repeatedly end at the process output level ("new policy created," "SharePoint set up," "clean point received") rather than the business outcome level ("reduced DPM by X%," "saved $Y in warranty," "freed Z hours/week").

The missing step in almost every story is the "So what?" chain — the candidate stops one level too early. Each story below identifies what that next level looks like.

The two best stories are MSD-02 (FLP Harness) and MSD-01 (FK Frame Fixture). Both should be the reference points for how to bring every other story up.

Intro Tell About Yourself
—
Self-Introduction Draft
★ Needs Full Rewrite NO METRICS ALL CLAIMS, NO EVIDENCE
What's Missing / Weak
  • "Self-driven," "passionate," "compartmentalize my thoughts" — all self-described traits with zero evidence. An interviewer will not believe these unless they're shown.
  • No promotions mentioned. No tenure. No scope of responsibility stated (how many machines? what programs?). No single headline result to anchor the intro.
  • "Quality / complexity / impact of the story shows the breadth..." — this is framing that should not be said out loud. Let the story prove it.
  • Rambling structure makes it hard to follow. A "Tell me about yourself" answer should be 90 seconds max, three beats: where you've been → what you've driven → why you're here.
How to Fix It
  • Start with a concrete anchor: title, tenure, scope. e.g., "I've been a Lead Electrical QE at John Deere for [X] years, supporting factory builds like the Mercury Grizzly and Caribou across electrical wire harness quality."
  • One headline accomplishment with a number: e.g., "Most recently I drove resolution of a supplier non-conformance that affected 100+ machines across two product lines, catching it before a single unit shipped to a customer."
  • Then bridge to the role: "I'm looking to bring that combination of technical depth and cross-functional leadership into [target role]."
  • Cut everything about "compartmentalizing thoughts," "irons in the fire," and "tail end of quality." These are distractions.
Bottom Line

This intro as written would not make a strong first impression. It's a working draft — the raw ingredients are there but it reads like notes, not a prepared answer. Needs to be rebuilt as a 90-second spoken structure with one anchor number and one forward-looking statement.
