★★ Verdict: "You Went Above and Beyond"

Your resume is already significantly stronger than most engineering resumes — you have real accomplishments with real numbers, and that puts you ahead of the vast majority. The gap between where you are and ★★★ is narrower than you might think. The main things holding you back are: (1) many of your best bullets try to pack two or three accomplishments into one sentence, which dilutes the punch of each, (2) several of your strongest metrics (70% defect reduction, 90% sensor issue reduction) stop short of translating the result into what it meant for the business in dollars or production uptime, and (3) heavy use of internal John Deere acronyms makes it hard for anyone outside your organization to quickly understand the value you delivered. Fixing these would likely push you past 70.

Key Issues Identified

1

Many bullets are doing double or triple duty — and each accomplishment loses impact because of it

Your first John Deere bullet is a great example: "Reduced repeat defects (CARs) by 70% year-over-year from 83 to 16 on the MY26 program... resolved 57 new non-conformances and dispositioned ~1,400 field automation events." That's actually two strong accomplishments crammed into one sentence. The 70% defect reduction deserves its own bullet where it can breathe and include the business impact. The 57 NCRs and 1,400 events is a separate achievement with its own scale. When you pack them together, a recruiter skimming for 6 seconds will absorb neither fully. This pattern shows up in at least 5 of your bullets — splitting them would immediately make each one land harder.

2

Internal jargon is making your resume harder to read than it needs to be

EPDP, FGR, DPAR, LPB, DTAC, HX, ISG, FEE-HD, CARs, MY25, MY26 — you use at least 11 internal acronyms throughout the resume. You know what these mean, and your current colleagues know, but a hiring manager at another company (or even another function at John Deere) won't. The strongest bullets on your resume — like the FGR/DPAR meeting consolidation that saved $30K/year — would land even harder if the reader didn't have to stop and decode the acronyms first. Where possible, spell out what the process is in plain engineering language and keep the acronym as a parenthetical if it's a recognized standard.

3

Your strongest quantified bullets stop short of the "Why" — the business impact

You reduced sensor-related repeat issues by 90% over 4 years. That's an outstanding metric. But the bullet doesn't tell the reader what that 90% reduction meant — how many field failures were prevented? How much warranty cost or unplanned downtime did this avoid? How many machines stayed in the field instead of coming back? The same gap shows up on your 70% CAR reduction bullet. You have the "what" and the "how" — the missing piece is the "so what?" that connects the metric to a business result. In several cases you likely know the answer or can estimate it conservatively.

4

No bullet sets up the problem before showing the result

Every bullet on your resume starts with the solution. This is fine — it's the standard resume format — but the Impact Bullet Builder's "Problem Context" principle says that framing the before state makes the after result feel much bigger. For example, your 75% frame rework reduction is strong on its own. But if the reader first understood that frames were requiring X hours of rework per unit, costing $X/week in production delays, and that this was an escalating problem — then the 75% reduction hits much harder. Even one sentence of context before the result changes how the accomplishment reads.

5

Early career bullets are significantly weaker than your recent work

Your John Deere, Pella, and J.B. Hunt bullets are strong — full of metrics and results. But the Research Assistant bullet ("Completed 3 major precision agriculture research projects... within time") and the Production Engineer bullet ("Increased manufacturing efficiency by 3%") drop back to the ★ level. For a 30+ year career, this is normal — but it creates an uneven reading experience. Consider condensing these older roles to 1 sentence each, or rewriting them to highlight the most impressive result from each period.

6

University/Extension role has a duty-description bullet that doesn't match the rest of your resume's quality

"Planned and executed educational and training programs on machinery and precision ag technologies at local, regional, state, and national level as a university's extension representative." — This is a role description, not an accomplishment. How many programs? How many people trained? Did your training lead to any measurable adoption, behavior change, or cost savings for the farmers you worked with? Your other bullets from this period (the $500K in grants, the published research) are strong. This one would either benefit from specific outcomes or could be consolidated into the grant/research bullet.

7

Summary bullet 4 describes how you work, not what you achieved

"Partnered with suppliers, design engineers, and cross-functional teams to ship complex programs on time while reducing field failures." — Your first three summary bullets are excellent: they each state a specific result with a metric. This fourth one describes your approach (partnering, cross-functional) rather than a result. It would be stronger as either a specific quantified claim (e.g., "Managed quality across [X] supplier relationships spanning [X] product lines") or replaced with another headline-worthy metric from your career.

Action Items

1

Split overloaded bullets into individual accomplishments

At least 5 of your bullets contain 2–3 distinct accomplishments packed into one sentence. Separate each one into its own bullet so each result can include its own metric, method, and business impact. This alone will make the resume read more clearly and give each achievement the room it deserves.

2

Add the "Why" to your strongest quantified bullets

Your 70% CAR reduction, 90% sensor issue reduction, and 20% absenteeism reduction bullets all have great metrics but stop short of saying what that meant for the business. Estimate the dollar impact, production uptime gained, or field failures prevented. Even conservative approximations make these bullets significantly stronger.

3

Replace internal acronyms with plain-language descriptions

Spell out EPDP, FGR, DPAR, LPB, DTAC, HX, ISG, FEE-HD, and CARs on first use — or replace them entirely with what the process/program actually is. A hiring manager outside John Deere should be able to understand every bullet without needing a decoder. Keep the acronym in parentheses if it's an industry-recognized standard.

4

Add problem context to your top 3 bullets

Pick the 3 accomplishments you're most proud of and add a brief "before" state. For example, before the 75% frame rework reduction — what was the situation? How much rework was happening? What was it costing? Setting up the problem makes the solution feel much bigger. You don't need to do this for every bullet — just the top 3.

5

Condense or strengthen early career entries

The Research Assistant and Production Engineer roles are noticeably weaker than your recent work. For a 30+ year career, consider either condensing each to a single strong bullet that shows the best result from that period, or removing the weaker role (Elpro) entirely. The space would be better used expanding your John Deere bullets with problem context and business impact.

6

Replace Summary bullet 4 with a quantified result

Your first three summary bullets are strong — they each lead with specific metrics. The fourth ("Partnered with suppliers, design engineers, and cross-functional teams to ship complex programs on time while reducing field failures") describes how you work rather than what you achieved. Replace it with another headline-worthy metric, or state the scope of your supplier/program management more specifically (e.g., "[X] supplier relationships across [X] product lines").

7

Show your engineering software tools in context

ANSYS, SolidWorks, Pro/E, ArcGIS, ENVI, and ERDAS IMAGINE are all listed in Technical Skills but don't appear in any bullet. If these are tools you used in your research or early engineering work, add a brief mention inside the relevant bullet (e.g., "using ArcGIS for spatial yield mapping"). If they're outdated, consider removing them — a shorter, fully contextualized skills list is stronger than a long one with tools you can't point to.

8

Fix the typo: "supplies" → "suppliers"

In the Quality Engineer (OFP) bullet: "driving corrective actions across supplies and cross-functional stakeholders" should read "suppliers." Small typos can undermine otherwise strong content.
