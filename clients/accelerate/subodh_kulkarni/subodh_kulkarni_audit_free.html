2 Key Issues Identified
1
Missing "Why" on your three biggest achievements
Your strongest bullets — 70% CAR reduction on MY26, 90% defect reduction on MY25, and 90% sensor defect elimination — all stop at the metric without stating the downstream business consequence. The IBB formula requires a stated "Why": what did the defect reduction prevent or enable? Warranty claim avoidance? Production schedule protection? Field failure cost reduction? Even an estimate with a qualifier closes the loop: "preventing an estimated $[X] in annual warranty exposure" is materially stronger than leaving the business significance to the reader's imagination. This gap affects roughly 8 of 18 bullets and is the highest-impact edit available.
"Reduced repeat defects (CARs) by 70% year-over-year from 83 to 16 on the MY26 program..." — strong metric, no stated downstream business outcome.
2
Problem context is nearly absent across all 18 bullets
Only 7 of 18 bullets include any framing of stakes, before/after comparison, or scale that explains why the result was difficult or significant. Without context, a strong result reads as assumed rather than earned. Adding one sentence per bullet — "On a $[X]M program with unresolved CARs across 3 prior phases..." or "After two years of recurring sensor field failures with no systemic fix in place..." — flips the read from "what you did" to "why it mattered that you specifically did it." This is the lowest-scoring criterion at 4/10 and the single highest-impact improvement available across the entire resume.
11 of 18 bullets contain no problem framing, before/after comparison, or scale statement of any kind.
3
Two duty-level bullets are pulling down an otherwise top-tier resume
The extension teaching bullet ("Planned and executed educational and training programs...") and the graduate research assistant bullet ("Completed 3 major precision agriculture research projects...") are the only ★-level entries on the page. They describe what you were doing, not what changed because you did it. At 30 years of experience, these are the entries most likely to be skimmed past or raise questions. Both need outcome-first rewrites or should be consolidated into a single aggregate impact statement with audience size, publication count, and adoption outcome.
"Planned and executed educational and training programs on machinery and precision ag technologies..." — no metrics, no audience size, no outcome stated.
4
NDE certifications are disconnected from where you used them
Your Level II NDE certifications (UT, MT, LP) are listed in the Certifications section — disconnected from the Walchandnagar bullet where you applied them on a nuclear power plant and defense fabrication project. Certifications in a list describe what you hold; certifications inside an experience bullet describe what you did with them. Moving the method names into the bullet — "applying ISNT/ASNT Level II NDE methods (UT, MT, LP) to achieve zero structural non-conformances" — directly connects credential to outcome and makes both stronger.
"Led QC/QA activities for a 6,000 metric ton fabricated nuclear power plant steel structure..." — NDE methods applied are listed separately in Certifications, not in the bullet where they were used.

5 Top Action Items
1
Add "Why" to the three flagship JD defect-reduction bullets
The MY26 (70% CAR), MY25 (90% reduction), and sensor (90% elimination) bullets all stop at the metric without closing the loop to business consequence. For each, add one clause: warranty claim avoidance, field failure cost reduction, or production schedule protection. This is the single highest-leverage edit on this entire resume and requires only one sentence per bullet.
2
Add problem context to your top 5 JD bullets
For the Program Quality Engineer bullets, add one framing sentence that establishes what you walked into: the state of the program before your involvement, the scale of what was at risk, or the history of the problem. Format: "On a $[X]M program with [X] CARs unresolved across prior phases..." This is the lowest-scoring criterion (4/10) and turns results from impressive to earned.
3
Rewrite or consolidate the two ★-level bullets at the bottom
The extension teaching and graduate research bullets are the only entries dragging consistency down. Both need outcome framing with at least one metric — audience size, publication count, adoption outcome. If those numbers aren't available, consolidate both into a single aggregate statement. One strong combined entry outperforms two weak separate ones at the bottom of a 30-year career resume.
4
Move your NDE certifications into the Walchandnagar bullet
Credentials in a certifications list describe what you hold. Credentials inside an experience bullet describe what you did with them. Add "applying ISNT/ASNT Level II NDE methods (UT, MT, LP)" directly into the nuclear fabrication bullet. This connects the credential to the context where it mattered, and makes both the bullet and the certification section stronger.
5
Fix the single weak action verb — "Estimated 50% reduction..."
Replace "Estimated 50% reduction of defects..." with "Reduced warranty-related defects by ~50%..." The tilde (~) is the clean, professional convention for signaling an estimate. The qualifier belongs after the action, not as the first word. This is a 30-second fix that removes the only action verb failure on the resume.
