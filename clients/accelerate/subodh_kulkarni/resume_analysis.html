Verdict: "The Achiever" Tier — Strong Foundation, Missing the Final Layer

This resume is significantly stronger than most. Subodh has real metrics — 70% defect reductions, 90% repeat issue elimination, $125K/year savings, $500K in grants, 350+ engineering hours saved. Those numbers are rare and valuable. The problem: they're buried in bullets that are 3–4 lines long, the "before" state is never painted (no Dragon Slayer framing), and most metrics aren't anchored to a dollar amount or CEO-level outcome. The resume also has 10 bullets for the current role — it should have 5 of the absolute best. With tighter editing, Dragon Slayer context, and dollar translation on the strongest metrics, this resume could reach 80–85.

2

Key Issues Identified

1

Bullets are too long — 3–4 lines each, trying to do too much

Almost every bullet crams 2–3 separate accomplishments into one sentence. Example: the MY26 bullet packs in 8 features, 9 engineers, EPDP gates, AND on-time release — that's 4 separate proof points fighting for attention in one sentence. The reader skims and retains none of it. Per the Impact Bullet Builder: one bullet = one accomplishment = one impact. Split the strongest metrics into their own bullets, cut the weakest ones entirely.

2

No Dragon Slayer framing on any bullet

"Reduced repeat defects by 70%" is a great metric — but the reader doesn't know what was broken before. Was there a crisis? Were defects climbing? Was a program at risk of missing launch? Per the Dragon Slayer technique: paint the monster first, THEN show how you slayed it. "Repeat defects on the MY26 program were trending 3x above the prior year; implemented systematic root cause tracking that reduced CARs by 70% YoY" — now the reader feels the weight of the result.

3

"Over the years" appears multiple times — vague and weakens the metric

"Reduced repeat issues by 50% over the years" and "achieving 90% repeat defect reduction over the years" — over HOW many years? 1 year? 5 years? This vague phrasing makes impressive numbers feel accidental rather than intentional. Replace with a specific timeframe: "within 18 months," "across 3 model years," "over a 2-year production cycle." Precision signals credibility.

4

Too many bullets on the current role — 10 is too many

The Program Quality Engineer role has 10 bullets. A hiring manager or recruiter spends 6–8 seconds per section. With 10 bullets, the strongest accomplishments (70% defect reduction, 350+ hours saved, 75% frame rework reduction) are competing for attention with weak bullets like "Successfully executed a special quality assurance project for 135 PMGNs" — which says nothing about the result. Keep 4–5 of the absolute best. Cut the rest ruthlessly.

5

"Helped identifying" — hedge language + grammatical error

"Helped identifying and escalated program risks..." is both grammatically incorrect ("helped identify") and uses hedge language. "Helped" is the weakest opener you can use — it signals you were adjacent to the work, not doing it. Per the Me-in-We Extractor: what specifically did YOU identify? What risk? What was the quantified impact? If the answer is "I found the sensor support failure mode that was going to delay Yield 3.0" — say that.

6

Summary section is generic claims without proof

"Known for execution, flexibility, and agility" and "Data driven decision maker with strong analytical skills" — these are BS Meter violations. Anyone can claim these traits. A summary should be the highlight reel: "Quality engineering leader with $125K+ in documented cost savings, 70–90% defect reductions across 4 combine programs, and $500K+ in secured research grants." Let the numbers prove the execution and the analytical skills.

7

Weak bullets taking up space alongside strong ones

"Successfully executed a special quality assurance project for 135 PMGNs" (what project? what result?), "Revised HX work instructions..." (task description with no metric), and "Planned and executed educational and training programs..." (no numbers, no impact). These dilute the resume. The $125K savings at Pella, the $250K bonus in India, and the 70%+ defect reductions at Deere — THOSE should dominate. Remove anything that can't answer "so what?"

8

Metrics exist but most aren't translated to dollars

"Saved 350+ engineering hours" — at what loaded rate? If an engineer's loaded cost is ~$80/hr, that's $28,000+/year in recovered capacity. "Reduced FEE frame rework by 75% (30 hrs/week saved)" — that's potentially $125K+/year in labor savings. The numbers are strong enough to translate — they just haven't been. A few dollar anchors on the biggest metrics would push this resume from Achiever to Impact Standard.
